## Background

Mike Diaz (J. Michael Diaz), 51, of Seattle, is a judge on Division I of the Washington Court of Appeals (2022-present); he previously served on the King County Superior Court (2018-2022), including as chief judge of the Clark Children and Family Justice Center [S1][S3]. Governor Jay Inslee appointed him to both benches [S1][S2]. Born in Peru and raised in South Seattle, he spent 10 years as an Assistant United States Attorney for the Western District of Washington, where he founded the office's Civil Rights Unit and helped lead Seattle Police Department reform efforts [S1][S2]. President Obama nominated him to the federal bench, but the nomination was never brought to a Senate vote [S1][S2]. He holds a JD from Cornell Law School, did graduate study in classical philosophy at Princeton, and earned a BA magna cum laude from Notre Dame; he is an adjunct constitutional law professor at Seattle University School of Law [S1].

## Positions

- Describes his judicial philosophy as "American pragmatism," holding that law must "meet the community where they are" — an adaptive, community-responsive approach [S3].
- Emphasizes judicial independence and integrity "at a time when the rule of law is under assault" [S1].
- Campaign message stresses protecting rights and freedoms with fairness and integrity ("Proven. Principled. Prepared.") [S2].

## Record

- Presided over 45 trials on the King County Superior Court [S1].
- Has authored over 200 opinions on the Court of Appeals, including, per his pamphlet, opinions protecting workers, holding corporations accountable, and addressing public safety [S1].
- Chairs the Washington Supreme Court's Interpreter Commission and serves on the Judicial Ethics Board [S1].
- As an AUSA, founded the Seattle U.S. Attorney's Office Civil Rights Unit focused on workplace, housing, and community protections [S1][S2].

## Endorsements

- Three former governors (Inslee, Gregoire, Locke), Attorney General Nick Brown, nine Supreme Court justices (five current and four retired), over one hundred judges, the King County Democrats and multiple county Democratic parties, the Washington State Labor Council/AFL-CIO, and the Washington Education Association [S1][S2][S3].
- Bar evaluations (self-reported): rated "Exceptionally Well Qualified" by Washington Women Lawyers, QLaw, the Latino/a Bar Association, and the Joint Asian Judicial Evaluation Committee (his pamphlet cites three such ratings; his website lists four) [S1][S2].
- Fundraising: over $220,000 raised as of early July 2026, the most in the race [S3].

## Scoring notes

- Only candidate in the race with appellate-bench experience, and the only one who currently decides appeals — the day-to-day work of a Supreme Court justice [S1][S3].
- Highest reported bar-association evaluations in the race ("Exceptionally Well Qualified" from multiple minority and specialty bar associations, self-reported) [S1][S2].
- Leads the race in both fundraising and establishment endorsements (former governors, most sitting justices), while chief rival Hawk holds the current governor's endorsement [S3].
- His "American pragmatism" philosophy contrasts with Stevens' strict constructionism and sits near Hawk's precedent-plus-reform framing [S3].
