## What this office does

The Washington Supreme Court is the state's court of last resort: nine justices elected statewide in nonpartisan races to six-year terms decide appeals that set binding precedent on the state constitution and state statutes, and the court oversees administration of the lower courts [S1][S2]. Although no party labels appear on the ballot, parties and elected officials endorse actively in these races [S2]. The top two finishers in the August 4 primary advance to November [S2].

## Race dynamics

Position 3 is an open seat: incumbent Justice Raquel Montoya-Lewis chose not to seek re-election, drawing three sitting judges into the race [S2]. All three currently serve on the bench, but at different levels and with sharply different paths: Court of Appeals Judge Mike Diaz (former federal civil-rights prosecutor, twice appointed by Governor Inslee), King County Superior Court Judge Jaime Hawk (former public defender and ACLU of Washington legal strategist, appointed by Inslee in 2022 and twice elected), and Mason County Superior Court Judge David Stevens (former prosecutor and public defender with international rule-of-law experience, elected rather than appointed) [S1][S2][S3]. The endorsement landscape splits three ways: Diaz has three former governors, the attorney general, and most current and retired justices; Hawk has sitting Governor Ferguson, Justice Whitener, retired Justice Yu, and 100+ judges; Stevens has the state, King County, and Snohomish County Republican parties [S1][S2][S3]. Fundraising as of early July: Diaz $220,000+, Hawk $163,000+, Stevens roughly $30,000 [S2].

## What genuinely differentiates these candidates

- **Judicial philosophy — the clearest axis:** Stevens is a self-described strict constructionist who would read statutes literally and interpret the constitution by text and original meaning; Diaz espouses "American pragmatism," law meeting "the community where they are"; Hawk stakes a middle framing of constitution, statute, and precedent "not personal views," with openness to reform [S2].
- **Level and type of bench experience:** Diaz is the only appellate judge (200+ opinions authored) and also served four years as a trial judge; Hawk and Stevens are trial judges, with Stevens claiming 200+ jury trials as lawyer and judge, and Hawk hundreds of cases presided plus appellate work from the trial bench [S1][S2].
- **Pre-bench careers point in different directions:** Diaz was a federal prosecutor who built a civil rights unit; Hawk was an indigent-defense and ACLU civil-liberties lawyer; Stevens worked both prosecution and defense, plus international justice missions in Kosovo and Afghanistan [S1][S2][S3].
- **Appointment vs. election and partisan alignment:** Stevens runs against the gubernatorial-appointment pipeline and holds Republican party recommendations; Diaz and Hawk were both appointed by Democratic governors and split the Democratic-aligned establishment (Diaz: former governors and most justices; Hawk: the sitting governor) [S1][S2][S3].
- **Bar evaluations:** Diaz reports "Exceptionally Well Qualified" ratings from multiple bar associations; no equivalent ratings were found for Hawk or Stevens in reviewed sources [S1][S2].
