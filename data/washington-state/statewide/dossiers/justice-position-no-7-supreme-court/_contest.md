## What the office does

The Washington State Supreme Court is the state's court of last resort: nine
justices who decide appeals from the Courts of Appeals, direct-review cases,
questions of state constitutional law, attorney-discipline matters, and rules
governing all state courts [S1][S2]. Justices are elected statewide in
nonpartisan races to six-year terms, with the top two primary finishers
advancing to November [S2][S3]. Position 7 additionally carries the court's
administrative leadership in practice: its incumbent is the current Chief
Justice, who presides over the court, co-chairs the Board for Judicial
Administration, and represents the judicial branch [S1][S4]. Five of nine
seats are on the 2026 ballot, with coverage focused on a pending
constitutional challenge to the state's new tax on incomes over $1 million
[S2][S3].

## Race dynamics

This is the most asymmetric of the 2026 Supreme Court races: incumbent Chief
Justice Debra L. Stephens — on the court since 2008, previously an Eastern
Washington Court of Appeals judge, and the verified author of the 2012
McCleary school-funding decision — faces three challengers, none of whom has
held judicial office [S1][S3][S4]. The challengers are Karim A. Merchant, a
former 15-year King County public defender now in solo practice; Todd A.
Bloom, a tax/business attorney (CPA, CFA, CFP), former FINRA arbitrator, Navy
veteran, and WSBA Board of Governors member; and David R Shelvey, a solo
family/tax lawyer from Sumner who ran for a different Supreme Court position
in 2024 and drew 3.4% in that primary [S4, and per candidate dossiers].

Institutional support is one-sided. Stephens is endorsed by all eight other
sitting justices, five retired justices, more than 150 judges, Governor
Ferguson, and the Washington State Labor Council [S3][S4, and per dossier].
The only countervailing action found is the Washington State Republican
Party's June 2026 "recommendation" of Bloom; Merchant and Shelvey list no
endorsements, with both framing that as a mark of independence [S4][S6, and
per dossiers]. Fundraising follows the same pattern: per PDC, Stephens
reported roughly $83,000 raised, Merchant roughly $10,000, Bloom roughly
$5,000, and Shelvey $0 [S5]. No 2026 bar-association judicial evaluations for
this race had been published as of 2026-07-16; ratings should be re-checked
closer to the election [S5, gap noted in all four dossiers].

## What genuinely differentiates the candidates

- **Judicial experience.** Stephens is the only candidate who has served as a
  judge — 18 years on the Supreme Court plus Court of Appeals service — and
  is the sitting Chief Justice [S1][S4]. Shelvey states he has no judicial
  experience; Merchant and Bloom likewise have none [S4].
- **Professional background of the challengers.** Merchant offers a criminal
  defense/constitutional-advocacy lens (15 years as a public defender, "never
  represented corporations, never represented the government"); Bloom offers
  a tax, securities, and business-law lens plus military service; Shelvey
  offers small-firm family/tax practice [S4].
- **Posture toward the court's recent rulings.** In Center Square Q&As, both
  Merchant and Bloom criticized the Quinn capital-gains-tax ruling, but they
  split on Blake: Merchant supported it, Bloom criticized it [S6]. Shelvey's
  pamphlet statement takes outcome-oriented positions (opposes a state income
  tax, "won't be soft on criminals"), which is itself unusual in a
  nonpartisan judicial race where candidates typically avoid prejudging
  issues [S4]. Stephens emphasizes judicial independence, access to justice,
  and deciding on law and facts [S4].
- **Scale and reach of campaigns.** Stephens has statewide institutional
  endorsements and the only substantial war chest; all three challengers are
  running low-budget campaigns, and one (Shelvey) reported no contributions
  at all [S5].
