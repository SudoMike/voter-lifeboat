## Background

Sharonda Amamilo, 60, of DuPont, is a Thurston County Superior Court judge, elected in 2020 as the first person of color to hold that seat and, per news coverage, the first African American judge in Thurston County history [S3][S2][S5]. She presides over criminal and civil jury trials, family and juvenile matters, child welfare cases, and administrative and lower-court appeals, and also sits as judge pro tempore on the Washington Court of Appeals, Division II [S1][S2]. Before the bench she spent twelve years at the Thurston County Office of Public Defense representing adults and children in criminal, juvenile, and dependency matters, and worked in private practice representing workers, families, small businesses, and youth in foster care [S1][S2]. She is a retired U.S. Army intelligence chief warrant officer with more than 25 years in military intelligence, including a deployment to Saudi Arabia during Operation Desert Storm [S1][S2][S4].

She holds a JD from Seattle University School of Law (attended at night), an MBA from Saint Martin's University, an undergraduate degree from Southern Illinois University, and a graduate certificate in data analytics from Cornell University (online) [S1][S3][S4]. She has served on the Washington State Sentencing Guidelines Commission and the Department of Children, Youth, and Families Oversight Board, chaired a grievance committee and participated in collective bargaining at Bates Technical College, and is board president of the Washington Microenterprise Association [S2][S4][S1]. In 2026 she is running for Position 5 against appointed incumbent Justice Theo Angelis, retired Federal Way Municipal Court Judge Dave Larson, and appellate attorney Greg Miller [S3].

## Positions

Framed as judicial philosophy and approach; this is a nonpartisan judicial office.

- Emphasizes adherence to precedent for stability: "We should not stray from the precedent that has helped create stability," while acknowledging a framework for revisiting precedent that is harmful or erroneous, subject to a "very high bar" [S4].
- Describes a fact-first analytical method she calls "FIRE" (Facts, Issues, Rules, Exceptions) and stresses textual precision in statutory interpretation ("Words matter. Some statutes say 'shall.' Some say 'must.'") [S4][S5].
- Argues the Supreme Court needs current trial-court perspective, noting "The Supreme Court does not get new facts" and citing her volume of trial-court decisions and weekly trial work [S3][S4].
- States her priorities are strengthening consistency and access to justice across Washington's 39 counties and protecting children and families in the dependency and foster-care system [S2][S1].
- On State v. Blake, says the decision lacked implementation guidance for trial courts and that she would have advocated for judicial input on rollout priorities and funding implications [S4].
- Pamphlet statement frames the justice's role as upholding the rule of law "with integrity, fairness, and respect for every individual," with decisions grounded in preparation, collaboration, and respect for the rule of law [S1].

## Record

- Has presided over adult felony trials (including violent crime, domestic violence, and drug cases), family law, dependency hearings, and complex civil litigation on the Thurston County Superior Court since 2021 [S2][S3].
- Conducts full record reviews and Administrative Procedure Act reviews, including as Court of Appeals judge pro tempore; coverage notes she has handled administrative appeals including multi-million-dollar tax disputes [S2][S3].
- News coverage reports she is known for scrutinizing bail and sentencing recommendations, particularly where disparities appear racially motivated [S5].
- Rated Exceptionally Well Qualified by the Washington State Veterans Bar Association, Pierce County Minority Bar Association, Loren Miller Bar Association, QLaw, and the Joint Asian Judicial Evaluations Committee; rated Well Qualified by the Latina/o Bar Association of Washington [S1][S2].
- Raised approximately $35,600 for this race as of early July 2026, third among the four Position 5 candidates [S3].

## Endorsements

- Sitting Supreme Court Justice Helen Whitener and former Justice Mary Yu [S3][S2].
- U.S. Rep. Marilyn Strickland (WA-10) and state Sens. Claire Wilson (LD 30) and Rebecca Saldaña (LD 37) [S2].
- Labor organizations including the Washington State Labor Council, SEIU Local 925, Laborers Local 292, and the Washington Education Association PAC [S2].
- Numerous county and legislative-district Democratic organizations (e.g., Snohomish, Skagit, Benton, Lewis, and Okanogan county Democrats; 11th, 27th, 30th, 36th, 37th, and 47th LD Democrats), plus groups including FairVote Washington, the National Women's Political Caucus of Washington, and the American Muslim Advancement Council [S2].
- Her campaign site also lists multiple state and county judges as endorsers [S2].

## Scoring notes

- Only candidate in this two-way comparison currently serving as a superior court judge, with felony, complex civil, family, and dependency jurisdiction plus appellate pro tem work; Larson's bench experience is longer but at the municipal-court level [S2][S3][S1].
- Carries the most extensive published bar-association evaluations in the race: Exceptionally Well Qualified from five minority and specialty bar associations, Well Qualified from a sixth [S1][S2].
- Endorsement base is anchored in sitting/former justices, Democratic organizations, and labor, contrasting with Larson's WAGOP recommendation and Forward Party endorsement; both offices are formally nonpartisan and partisan inference is inappropriate [S2][S3].
- Significant fundraising gap: roughly $35,600 versus Larson's ~$232,000 and incumbent Angelis's ~$277,300 as of early July 2026 [S3].
- Distinctive non-legal background: 25+ years in Army military intelligence and public-defense career, which she frames as "systems" expertise for improving court administration statewide [S3][S4].
