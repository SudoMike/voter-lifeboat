## Background

Dave Larson, 68, of Federal Way, is a retired trial judge who served on the Federal Way Municipal Court from 2008 to 2025, including roughly 16 years as presiding judge [S3][S4]. He reports 41 years of legal experience: 18 years on the bench and 23 years as a civil trial lawyer, plus work as a mediator and arbitrator from 1990 to 2008 [S1]. Before taking the bench he owned a law firm (1991–2001) and a mediation company (1998–2001) [S4]. He holds a BA from the University of Puget Sound and a JD from Seattle University School of Law, which he completed at night while working full-time [S1][S4][S2]. He served as president of the Federal Way School Board, resigning when he became a judge in 2008 [S1][S4]. He is rated AV Preeminent by Martindale-Hubbell, the peer-review rating service's highest attorney rating [S1][S2].

This is his fourth run for the state Supreme Court: he ran unsuccessfully in 2016, 2020, and 2024, losing the 2024 race by fewer than 20,000 votes (about 0.61 percent) after receiving over 1.6 million votes [S4][S2]. In 2026 he is running for Position 5 against appointed incumbent Justice Theo Angelis, Thurston County Superior Court Judge Sharonda Amamilo, and appellate attorney Greg Miller [S4].

## Positions

Framed as judicial philosophy and approach; this is a nonpartisan judicial office.

- Argues the Supreme Court needs a justice with substantial trial-court experience, saying appellate judges lack perspective when making court rules without practical bench time: "If you haven't actually done it, it's very hard to sit in judgment of judges" [S4].
- Describes courts as "the safety valve for society" and judges as keepers of the peace who should not legislate from the bench even when they personally disagree with a law [S4][S5].
- Says he came out of retirement because he views the current court as "a ship lost at sea" lacking stability, and wants the court to better understand how its decisions affect trial courts, lawyers, and communities [S5].
- Criticizes the court's State v. Blake drug-possession decision as "motivated by ideology," and says his campaign theme is restoring trust and judicial independence by rising above partisan interests [S5][S1].
- Opposes proposed bail rules he says would "cripple" trial courts by making warrant issuance nearly impossible, and argues public-defender caseload standards treat symptoms rather than root causes, potentially worsening defender shortages in Eastern Washington [S5].
- Warns that filling the court through gubernatorial appointments undermines judicial independence [S4].
- States he has collaborated with legislative Democrats and Republicans on trial-court approaches to crime and addiction combining "compassion and accountability" [S1].

## Record

- Presided over Federal Way Municipal Court 2008–2025; the campaign states he managed over 270,000 criminal and court filings and was retained by voters four times [S2][S3].
- Served on the District and Municipal Court Judges Association (DMCJA) Therapeutic Courts Committee (2019–2025), and on DMCJA legislative, judicial independence, and public trust and confidence committees; chaired the Council on Independent Courts (2023–2024) [S3].
- Received the DMCJA President's Award in 2018, which his pamphlet statement describes as recognizing protection of judicial independence [S1][S3].
- Taught new judges at the state judicial college for about 10 years and coordinated "Judges in the Classroom" in Southwest Washington; received the Washington Judges' Foundation Judge William Nevins Award (2014) for civics education work with youth [S2][S3][S1].
- Served as legislative liaison to a state House workgroup on impaired driving and on a legislative Municipal Court Task Force (1999–2000) [S3].
- As Federal Way School Board president, initiated a 2006 school-funding lawsuit targeting structural education-finance problems [S3].
- Raised approximately $232,000 for this race as of early July 2026, second among the four Position 5 candidates behind incumbent Angelis [S4].

## Endorsements

- Recommended by the Washington State Republican Party (April 2026) [S4].
- Endorsed by the Forward Party, co-chaired by Andrew Yang and former New Jersey Gov. Christine Todd Whitman [S4].
- His campaign website does not publish a list of individual endorsers [S2].

## Scoring notes

- Longest judicial tenure in the field on paper (18 years), but at the municipal-court level (misdemeanors, infractions) rather than superior-court felony/civil trial work or appellate work [S1][S4].
- Only candidate in the race openly critical of the current court's direction, including the Blake decision and proposed bail rules; runs on restoring "trust" and judicial independence [S5][S1].
- Repeat statewide candidate with demonstrated name recognition: three prior Supreme Court runs, including a 0.61 percent loss in 2024 [S4][S2].
- Endorsement pattern differs sharply from his opponent's: WAGOP recommendation and Forward Party versus Amamilo's Democratic-organization and labor endorsements; note both offices are formally nonpartisan and partisan inference is inappropriate [S4].
- Second-highest fundraising in the four-way primary (~$232,000), well ahead of Amamilo (~$35,600) [S4].
