U.S. Representative U.S. Representatives must be at least 25 years of age, have been a citizen of the U.S. for at least seven years, and be a registered voter of the state from which he or she is elected. Each Representative’s term is two years. The House of Representatives is made up of 435 members, with each state allocated a different number of members based on population. The total membership of the House is up for election in even-numbered years. The Senate and House have equal responsibility for declaring war, maintaining the armed forces, assessing taxes, borrowing money, minting currency, regulating commerce, and making all laws necessary for the operation of government. State Senator A senator’s term is four years. The Senate is made up of 49 members, one from each legislative district in the state. One half the membership of the Senate is up for election each even-numbered year. The Senate’s only exclusive duty is to confirm appointments made by the governor. During legislative sessions, the Legislature is called upon to enact or reject legislation affecting public policy in the state, provide for the levy and collection of taxes and other revenue to support state government and assist local government, and appropriate funds for these purposes. State Representative A representative’s term is two years. The House is made up of 98 members, two from each legislative district in the state. The total membership of the House is up for election each even-numbered year. During legislative sessions, the Legislature is called upon to enact or reject legislation affecting public policy in the state, provide for the levy and collection of taxes and other revenue to support state government and assist local government, and appropriate funds for these purposes. Supreme Court Justice Supreme Court Justices serve six-year terms. There are nine justices on the state Supreme Court. Three justices are up for election every two years and are voted on statewide. The Supreme Court is the highest judiciary in the state, hearing appeals and deciding on cases from the Court of Appeals and other lower courts. County Prosecuting Attorney The county prosecuting attorney is a partisan position and serves a four-year term. The county prosecuting attorney appears for and represents the county in actions and proceedings before the courts and judicial officers. Responsibilities include but are not limited to: Prosecuting criminal actions referred by law enforcement agencies; serving as legal adviser to county offices, departments and the county council; defending suits brought against the county; attending, appearing before and giving advice to grand juries; managing the administration of the prosecuting attorney’s office; recommending reforms to improve the administration of the criminal justice system; and providing advocacy services for crime victims and facilitating the testimony of crime victims. Public Utility District Commissioner Public Utility District (PUD) Commissioners serve six-year terms. PUD commissioners sit on the governing board of the public utility district. Only voters within a commissioner’s district may vote to nominate a candidate during the primary for the general election ballot. All voters in the entire public utility district vote to elect the commissioner in the general election. Responsibilities include but are not limited to: Setting district utility policies and priorities; appointing the utility district manager; providing oversight of the district’s operations; adopting system plans; establishing utility charges and rates; and representing members of their districts in public utility decision-making. Precinct Committee Officer A Precinct Committee Officer (PCO) is the representative of a political party at the grassroots level. PCO candidates do not appear in the Local Voters’ Pamphlet and are only on the ballot if more than one person from a political party files in a precinct. PCOs serve two-year terms. PCO candidates do not appear in the voters' pamphlet. What Does an Elected Official Do? Voting in Snohomish County

7 Return your ballot by August 4. A primary winnows the field of candidates ahead of the General Election if more than two candidates file for the same nonpartisan position. Partisan offices appear on the Primary ballot even if only one candidate files. The office of Precinct Committee Office (PCO) appears only on the Primary ballot and is decided in that election if two or more people file for the same precinct and party. Uncontested PCO candidates are deemed elected and do not appear on the ballot. The State of Washington has a Top 2 Primary system. This allows voters to choose among all candidates running for each office regardless of their party preference. The two candidates who receive the most votes in the Primary qualify for the General Election. Candidates need at least 1% of the votes cast in their race to advance to the General Election. Each candidate for a partisan office may state a political party that he or she prefers. A candidate’s preference does not imply that they are nominated or endorsed by the party, or that the party approves of or associates with that candidate. The order in which names appear on the Primary ballot is determined by lot draw. The 2026 lot draw order can be viewed at snoco.gov/elections. Candidates in the Primary who move on to the General Election will be ordered on the General Election ballot according to votes received in the Primary. Primary vs. General Election What's the Difference? August Primary November General A 17-year-old who will be 18 by the November General Election can vote for candidates in the August Primary.
