What's on the Ballot

The sample ballot contains all the candidates and/or measures that will be voted on in Snohomish County. It may look different from the official ballot you receive in the mail, which has only the candidates and/or measures you are eligible to vote on.

  • Sample Ballot (PDF)

Finding Information on Candidates and Measures

Your voters’ guide is the best place to get information on the candidates and measures that appear on your ballot. The voters’ guide lets candidates tell you who they are and what they stand for. Explanations of bond and levy measures, as well as statements for and against each measure, are also included in the voters' guide.

The voters' guide is available in two formats:

  • Online Voters’ Guide (VoteWA)

    Contains only the candidates and issues on your ballot. Once you log in, click on the "Voters’ Guide" tab on the left of your screen. 

  • Local Voters' Pamphlet

    Includes information on candidates and/or measures that will be voted on in Snohomish County. Every household is mailed one Local Voters' Pamphlet in primary and general elections. It is mailed either as an insert with the ballot or separately to households for special elections.

    View the Local Voters' Pamphlet (PDF)

  • League of Women Voters' Candidate Forums

    These nonpartisan candidate forums, hosted by the Snohomish County League of Women Voters, provide an opportunity for voters to hear directly from candidates in contested races as they respond to questions from a moderator. Voters can tune in for the live streams or view the recordings.

Ballot Resolutions Submitted by Districts

Local ballot measures are placed on the ballot by local districts. To do this, the district must submit a resolution to the Snohomish County Auditor's Office calling for an election. The district also provides an explanatory statement and identifies people to write statements in support of and in opposition to the measure. This information is used to prepare the local voters' pamphlet and place the measure on the ballot.

The full resolution does not appear on the ballot. Ballot language is developed separately and may differ from the wording used in the resolution.

Common questions about ballot measures (click or tap to expand):

  1. What are ballot measures?

A ballot measure is a question that voters are asked to approve or reject in an election. Local districts use ballot measures to make decisions or raise funding for services and projects. A short summary for each measure is included on the ballot, and the full details are provided in the voters' pamphlet. There are different types of measures:

  • Levy: Usually provides funding for ongoing local services like schools, fire protection, emergency medical services, hospitals, libraries, and more. School levies only need a simple majority of "yes" votes, while others require a 60% supermajority. Levy measures expire after a set number of years, and voter approval is required to renew the levy.

  • Bond: Allows a district to borrow funds for major capital projects, like building schools or public facilities. Funds are repaid over time through property taxes. Bonds always require a 60% supermajority and a minimum voter turnout. 

  • Other measures: Can include sales taxes, levy lid lifts, and non-revenue measures that require voter approval. 

Some measures are placed on the ballot through petition initiatives or referenda. Learn more about how these work on our Petitions for Initiatives and Referenda page.

  1. How are ballot measures passed?

Not all ballot measures are approved the same way. Some only need a simple majority of more than 50% of the votes to pass, while others have a higher threshold of requirements or additional rules. Here's a breakdown:
 

  • Simple Majority Measures: Most measures, like sales taxes, school levies, and levy lid lifts, pass if more than 50% of voters vote "yes." No minimum voter turnout is required.

  • Supermajority Measures: Other measures require at least 60% "yes" votes, or a supermajority, to pass. 

  • Turnout Rules: Certain measures also require a minimum number of voters to participate, based off voter turnout in the last general election:

  • Bonds: Must receive 60% "yes" votes and at least 40% turnout of the voters who participated in the last general election. So, a bond measure could receive the required number of "yes" votes and still fail if not enough people vote in the election.

  • Levy ballot measure: Must receive at least 60% “yes” votes and meet validation requirements based on voter participation in the most recent general election. Validation is met either through sufficient voter turnout or by meeting a minimum number of “yes” votes set by law.
     
  • School Levies: Only a simple majority of votes cast is required.

For more detailed information, see Validation Requirements (PDF). Specific details about the ballot measures that appear on your ballot can be found in the voters' pamphlet. If you have additional questions, please reach out to the district's representative.

  1. If a levy or bond passes, how will it affect my taxes and local services?

When a levy/bond passes, the impact on the services your community receives and the taxes you may pay depends greatly on your unique situation. For the information on proposed uses for funding measures, see your voters’ guide or reach out to your district representative. You can find your elected officials on VoteWA.gov or by using our interactive district map.

For information on how your current property taxes are distributed, please visit the Snohomish County Assessor’s website.

Candidates on the Ballot

Candidate filing takes place during the first full week of May. This is when individuals officially submit their paperwork to run for federal, state, or local office.

After candidate filing closes, a primary election is held in August to narrow the field of candidates before the General Election in November. 

Nonpartisan offices: Candidates appear on the primary ballot only if more than two people filed for the position. If two or fewer candidates filed for the position, they advance directly to the General Election.

Partisan offices: Candidates running for partisan offices always appear on the primary ballot, even if only one or two candidates filed for the position. A partisan office is a government position in which candidates may specify a political party preference, but their stated preference does not mean the party endorses or approves the candidate. 

To view the offices that affect your area, enter your address in our Find Your District and Elected Officials interactive map.

Common questions about candidates on the ballot (click or tap to expand):

  1. Why don't I see a particular candidate on my ballot?

We use your residential address to determine the candidates and measures that appear on your ballot. You can check your local, state, and federal districts and elected officials using our interactive district map.

In a primary election, candidates who have filed for a partisan position will always appear on the primary ballot, regardless of how many people filed to run for that position. Candidates who have filed for a nonpartisan position appear on the primary ballot only if more than two candidates filed for that position. Otherwise, they will advance directly to the general election in November.

  1. Where can I find the descriptions and responsibilities of elected offices?

We provide descriptions of all elected positions on the ballot in the Local Voters' Pamphlet for the election. You can also find qualifications and responsibilities for federal and state elected offices on the Secretary of State's website. 

  1. Why aren't candidate names displayed in alphabetical order?

As required by state law, the order that candidates' names appear on the ballot and in the Local Voters' Pamphlet is determined by a random lot drawing, which is held by staff at the end of filing week. During this process, staff and a member of the public pull lettered tiles from a rotating "bingo barrel." The order that the lettered tiles are drawn in is recorded and becomes the official candidate listing order.

The lot drawing is open to the public to observe, and the final listing order is publicly available on our Run for Office page.
