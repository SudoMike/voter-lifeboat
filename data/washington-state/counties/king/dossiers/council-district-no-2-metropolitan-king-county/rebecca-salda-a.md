## Background

Rebecca Saldaña has been the state senator for the 37th Legislative
District (South Seattle, Central District, Beacon Hill, Rainier Valley,
Renton, Skyway) since her appointment in 2016; she chairs the Senate Labor
& Commerce Committee and sits on Agriculture & Natural Resources, Rules,
and Ways & Means [S1][S3]. Before the legislature she was executive
director of Puget Sound Sage (2014–2016), a nonprofit working on
affordable housing, transportation equity, environmental justice, and
workers' rights, and organized with the farmworker unions PCUN and United
Farm Workers and with SEIU Local 6 [S1][S3]. She is the daughter of a
formerly undocumented Mexican farmworker, grew up in Seattle's Delridge
neighborhood, and holds a B.A. in humanities and theology from Seattle
University [S1][S3].

## Positions

- **Housing:** Wants to build more housing "affordable at any income";
  at a June 2026 forum she was reticent about a county property-tax levy
  for social housing, preferring progressive revenue options and a
  state-level approach [S1][S4].
- **Taxes:** Campaigns on taxing the ultra-wealthy and large corporations
  to fund services; says she championed the capital gains tax and a
  "Millionaires Tax" in the senate (her site describes her as a
  co-sponsor of the capital gains tax) [S1][S2].
- **Transit:** Wants transit "fast, reliable, and free"; supported the idea
  of a countywide transit levy at the forum, though with a less definitive
  stance than her opponent on service-versus-electrification trade-offs
  [S2][S4].
- **Childcare/labor:** Supports universal childcare, raising wages, and a
  $25 minimum wage push; cites Paid Family and Medical Leave and the
  Domestic Workers Bill of Rights as her model [S1][S2].
- **Immigration/civil rights:** Pledges to protect communities from ICE
  enforcement actions and wage theft, and to insulate the county from
  federal cuts [S1][S4].
- **Behavioral health/public safety:** Says she will expand behavioral
  health and crisis response [S1].

## Record

- Appointed to the state senate in 2016; Senate Labor & Commerce Committee
  chair [S3].
- Prime-sponsored and passed the HEAL Act (first statewide environmental
  justice law), the Washington Voting Rights Act, the Domestic Workers
  Bill of Rights, and the Adult Entertainers Bill of Rights [S3].
- Campaign claims contributions to the capital gains tax (co-sponsor),
  Paid Family and Medical Leave, the Climate Commitment Act, the Clean
  Fuel Standard, and the Move Ahead Washington transportation package
  (campaign characterizations of shared legislative efforts) [S1][S2].
- Campaign fundraising: about $208,467 in reported contributions for the
  council race as of mid-July 2026, slightly ahead of Hasegawa; she also
  has a separate state-senate campaign account [S6].

## Endorsements

- Sole endorsement of the King County Democrats (pamphlet claim), and MLK
  Labor's endorsement for this seat (verified) [S1][S5].
- King County Councilmembers Sarah Perry (chair), Teresa Mosqueda, and
  Claudia Balducci; former Councilmember Larry Gossett [S1][S2].
- Seattle officials: City Attorney Erika Evans, Council President Joy
  Hollingsworth, Councilmembers Dionne Foster, Eddie Lin, Rob Saka; former
  Councilmember Debora Juarez; former U.S. Rep. Jim McDermott [S2].
- Over 30 state legislators, including Senators Wellman, Pedersen,
  Dhingra, and Valdez [S2].
- Labor: SEIU 775, SEIU 925, UFCW 3000, PROTEC17, Teamsters 28/117/174/763,
  AFSCME Council 2, IAM 751, UAW 4121 (graduate students) [S1].
- Community leaders including Estela Ortega (El Centro de la Raza) and
  Sheley Secrest (NAACP Washington) [S2].

## Scoring notes

- A decade-long state legislative record with several passed laws she
  prime-sponsored — the deepest legislative paper trail in this race
  [S3].
- Prefers progressive-revenue and state-level tools over a county
  social-housing property-tax levy, the sharpest policy contrast with
  Hasegawa [S4].
- Holds the institutional Democratic Party lane (sole King County
  Democrats endorsement) and the service-worker/public-employee union
  lane, versus Hasegawa's maritime/trades base [S1][S5].
- At the June 2026 forum she leaned on her record "standing up to
  corporate interests" in Olympia, while Hasegawa countered with
  project-delivery experience at the Port — legislative versus executive
  experience is the core stylistic contrast [S4].
