## What this office does

A Washington State Representative is one of two House members elected from each legislative district to a two-year term in the part-time state Legislature in Olympia (salary about $72,494/year [S2]). Representatives write and vote on state law and the state operating, capital, and transportation budgets — meaning K-12 school funding, state taxes (property tax caps, B&O tax, the capital gains/"millionaires" tax), housing and land-use law, Medicaid/Apple Health, higher-education tuition policy, climate and energy policy, and public-safety statutes. LD32 spans northwest Seattle, Shoreline, Woodway, Edmonds, Mountlake Terrace, and Lynnwood, crossing the King/Snohomish county line [S3].

## Race dynamics

- **Open seat.** Sixteen-year incumbent Rep. Cindy Ryu (D-Shoreline) is vacating the seat to challenge Sen. Jesse Salomon for the LD32 Senate seat [S2][S3]. Six candidates filed: five Democrats (Chris Bloomquist, Will Chen, Jenna Nand, Danica Noble, Keith Scully) and one Republican (Lisa Rezac) [S1][S2].
- **Top-two primary math.** With five Democrats splitting the vote in a strongly Democratic district and one Republican consolidating the minority vote, Rezac could plausibly reach the general despite raising only ~$4,000; the real contest is which Democrat advances [S2][S4].
- **Fundraising** (PDC, as of 2026-07-16): Noble $114,947; Scully $61,868; Bloomquist $58,163; Chen $52,361; Nand $33,346; Rezac $4,058 [S4].
- **Endorsement landscape is split.** The 32nd District Democrats made no primary endorsement in this race [S5]. Scully has both newspaper editorial boards (Seattle Times, The Stranger) and retiring Rep. Ryu [S2][S3]; Noble has the Washington State Labor Council and a bloc of legislators [S1][S2]; Bloomquist has both sitting LD32 legislators (Salomon, Davis — who recruited him) [S3]; Nand has WEA-PAC and Snohomish County Executive Somers [S1]; Chen's support comes mostly from local mayors and councilmembers [S2].
- **Incumbency adjacent.** Three candidates are sitting city councilmembers (Chen and Nand in Edmonds; Scully in Shoreline, a former mayor); Noble and Bloomquist are first-time candidates with professional-expertise pitches [S1][S2][S3].

## Differentiating issue axes

1. **Tax approach.** Scully: restructure the tax code progressively, tax business profit not revenue, plus across-the-board budget cuts sparing education. Nand: repeal the 1% property tax cap, has floated a flat income tax, "progressive revenue." Chen: lift the 1% property tax cap but self-described financially "careful." Bloomquist: keep the millionaires tax, no new taxes on working families. Rezac: no new taxes, cut waste [S2][S3]. This is the sharpest policy divide among the Democrats.
2. **Governing experience vs. outside expertise.** Municipal records (Chen's budget/finance work, Scully's housing/transit/mayoral record, Nand's council tenure) versus enforcement/industry expertise (Noble's FTC antitrust career, Bloomquist's clean-energy recruiting) [S1][S2][S3].
3. **Technology/AI regulation.** Noble makes tech guardrails, ed-tech data limits, school cellphone bans, and AI rules her core platform; Bloomquist pledges AI child-safety legislation; Scully lists "managing AI"; others treat it lightly [S1][S2][S3].
4. **Education funding mechanics.** Noble and Bloomquist both propose moving off per-student head-count funding; Scully wants urban cost adjustments; Nand emphasizes tuition subsidies [S2][S3].
5. **Party direction (general-election axis).** Rezac runs explicitly against one-party control on accountability, parental rights, and public safety — the only candidate on that side of the divide [S1][S2].
