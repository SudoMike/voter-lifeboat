## Background

Al Dams is the current Chief Deputy Assessor of King County, a position he describes as the number-two role in the office, which he has held for 11 years [S1]. He holds a Bachelor of Arts in Communication from the University of Washington, cum laude [S1]. His campaign website states he has more than 30 years of experience in King County government, with leadership roles in the departments of Assessments, Parks, Executive Services, and Public Works [S2]. He is married with five children [S2]. He served as Chief Deputy under Assessor John Wilson [S4].

## Positions

- **Property tax exemptions:** Says he is committed to expanding property tax exemptions so that people "of all ages" are eligible, building on senior exemption work he cites from his current role [S1].
- **Accuracy and lawfulness of levies:** Emphasizes reviewing funding requests from schools, fire departments, and cities for accuracy and lawfulness, stating that errors overtax families and underfund services [S1].
- **Continuity and technical expertise:** Frames the race as a choice between experienced in-office leadership and candidates who "must learn it from scratch"; his campaign emphasizes knowledge of mass appraisal systems, data management, GIS, valuation modeling, and the office's IT infrastructure [S1][S2].
- **Office standards:** Presents maintaining the office's professional standards, including its Certificate of Excellence in Assessment Administration, as a goal of his candidacy [S1][S2].

## Record

- As Chief Deputy Assessor, he states he leads the property-valuation effort covering roughly 750,000 residential and commercial properties annually and manages teams of appraisers, analysts, and technologists [S1].
- He states he worked to reform property tax exemption programs so that thousands of additional seniors receive tax breaks to stay in their homes (self-reported; not independently verified in sources reviewed) [S1].
- He states the office earned the Certificate of Excellence in Assessment Administration, which he says has been achieved by 65 jurisdictions in the U.S. and Canada and two in Washington state (self-reported) [S1].
- In May 2025, when the King County Democrats chair called for an investigation into whether Assessor John Wilson had used county property data amid stalking and harassment allegations involving Wilson, Dams, as chief deputy, stated that no investigation had taken place [S4].
- Campaign finance: PDC records as of 2026-07-16 show itemized contributions totaling approximately $45,860 across 78 records and expenditures totaling approximately $34,448 for his 2026 County Assessor campaign [S3].

## Endorsements

- In his pamphlet statement, Dams says he is endorsed by "dozens of people," naming King County Councilmember Claudia Balducci, former Deputy County Executive Fred Jarrett, State Representative Kristine Reeves, and Bothell City Councilmember Prasad Anguluri (self-reported) [S1].
- His campaign website did not display an endorsement list at the time of access; it contains a form for submitting endorsements [S2].
- The Seattle Times editorial board endorsed his opponent Rob Foxcurran in this race [S5].

## Scoring notes

- Only candidate in the race currently serving inside the Assessor's office; his case rests on 11 years as Chief Deputy and operational continuity [S1][S2].
- His stated policy agenda is narrower than some opponents': expanding exemption eligibility and levy-review accuracy, versus opponent proposals for state-level tax structure changes [S1].
- He served as second-in-command under Assessor John Wilson, who faced resignation calls in 2025 over stalking and harassment allegations; Dams's documented public role in that episode was stating that no internal investigation into data-misuse allegations had occurred [S4].
- Claimed endorsements are individual elected and former officials; major institutional endorsements found in coverage of this race (Seattle Times editorial board) went to an opponent [S1][S5].
