## What the office does

A Washington State Representative is one of 98 members of the state House, elected to a two-year term (each legislative district elects two representatives and one senator). Representatives write and vote on state law, adopt the operating, capital, and transportation budgets, and set state tax policy. Unlike the four-year Senate seat, House members face voters every cycle, so this race re-tests the incumbent less than a year after he won a special election [S2].

## District and dynamics

The 33rd District covers SeaTac, Normandy Park, Des Moines, and parts of Burien, Kent, Renton, and unincorporated King County; Democrats hold all three of its legislative seats [S2][S3]. Incumbent Democrat Edwin Obras was appointed in December 2024 (when Rep. Tina Orwall moved to the Senate) and narrowly won the November 2025 special election over a more moderate fellow Democrat, Burien Mayor Kevin Schilling, by 641 votes [S2]. In 2026 he instead faces two Republicans — Darryl K. Jones of Kent and Chris Martinez — meaning the top-two primary will almost certainly send Obras plus one Republican to the general, with the primary effectively deciding which Republican advances [S1][S3]. The resource gap is stark: Obras raised roughly $253k for 2025 and $18.5k so far for 2026; Martinez has about $5.5k and Jones has no itemized PDC filings [S4]. Last year's close special election reflected an intra-Democratic moderate-vs-progressive split; with no moderate Democrat on this ballot, that dynamic does not carry over directly [S2].

## Differentiating issue axes

- **Record vs. no record:** Obras has six bills signed into law and a leadership post; both challengers are first-time candidates whose only political experience is precinct committee officer [S1].
- **Affordability diagnosis:** All three campaign on cost of living, but with opposite prescriptions — Obras via rent caps, worker protections, and state investment; Jones and Martinez via lower taxes and reduced state spending [S1].
- **Climate/energy policy:** Martinez explicitly targets the Climate Commitment Act for elimination; Obras's coalition includes Washington Conservation Action; Jones is silent on the topic [S1].
- **Social issues:** Martinez runs on parental rights, "traditional family values," and religious themes; Jones confines himself to fiscal issues; Obras emphasizes immigrant rights — a clean three-way spread for scoring [S1].
- **Within the Republican lane:** Martinez has the King County GOP endorsement, a specific bill-by-bill platform, and some fundraising; Jones has a one-theme fiscal message and no visible campaign infrastructure [S1][S4].
