## Background

Silas James is a disabled educator, researcher, and advocate, and a former Chair of the Seattle Disability Commission [S1]. He holds a B.A. in Political Science from The Evergreen State College and a Master of Public Administration from the UW Evans School, and works as a research coordinator [S1]. He describes nearly two decades of research and advocacy work with vulnerable populations, including at the University of Washington; he uses a wheelchair and relies on public transit, and describes long personal experience navigating public benefit systems [S1][S2][S4]. He runs as a self-described democratic socialist [S2][S4].

## Positions

### Taxes and income support
- Says wealthy corporations should "pay their fair share"; proposes taxing wealthy corporations to fund community resilience and safety systems [S1][S2].
- Proposes a guaranteed basic income of $500 per month for households earning below 300% of the federal poverty level, a plan he says he has submitted to the City Council and Mayor [S2][S4].
- Proposes a vacancy tax on residential and commercial properties, citing empty storefronts such as the former Bartell Drugs building [S3][S4].

### Housing and homelessness
- Advocates social housing reaching 30-40% of Seattle's housing stock [S4].
- Supports "carefully increasing density while protecting the character of our neighborhoods and public spaces" [S2].
- Supports housing-first solutions, opposes encampment sweeps, backs tiny home villages, and supports case management and safe camping/parking spaces [S3].
- Supports tenant unions with enforceable penalties for landlords who refuse to bargain, and wants tenants designated a protected class under the Seattle Office for Civil Rights [S3].

### Public safety and surveillance
- Frames poverty and wealth disparity as the primary public safety threats rather than a need for increased police presence; supports restorative justice over incarceration for nonviolent offenses [S4].
- Supports expanding the CARE team with independent dispatch authority, freeing it from police-required accompaniment on mental health calls [S3][S4].
- Opposes surveillance tools including automatic license plate readers, CCTV, and the city's Axon contracts (which he says cost about $8 million a year and criticizes for the company's ICE connections), arguing they "have few public safety benefits, but put many of us at risk" [S2][S3][S4].

### Immigration
- Proposes limiting SPD and city cooperation with ICE, divesting from ICE-affiliated companies, and removing SPD from federal task forces [S2].

### Transit, climate, and accessibility
- Supports "fast, reliable, and free public transportation," dedicated bus lanes, protected bike lanes, and better east-west service in District 5, plus green jobs development [S2][S4].
- Emphasizes universal design: accessible crossings with drop curbs, neighborhood hubs, and rapid transit connectivity [S3].

### Small business
- Proposes resuming the Seattle Restored program for pop-up markets in vacant storefronts [S3].

## Endorsements

- Hacks & Wonks reports that Dorothy Bullitt endorsed him, which he characterized as her first endorsement of a socialist candidate [S4]. No organizational endorsements were found on his campaign website or in the coverage reviewed [S2].

## Campaign finance

- PDC records show about $45,444 in reported contributions (230 itemized entries) for the 2026 cycle as of 2026-07-16 — roughly 40% of what Jenks or Kang report, but from a comparable number of contributions [S5].
- Participates in the Democracy Voucher Program; has "Completed Qualifying Process" and may collect and redeem vouchers [S6].

## Scoring notes

- The furthest-left platform in this race: a self-described democratic socialist proposing guaranteed basic income, social housing at 30-40% of stock, vacancy taxes, and corporate taxation [S2][S4].
- The most explicit opponent of surveillance technology and the Axon contract in the field, and the only candidate proposing independent dispatch authority for the CARE team [S2][S3][S4].
- Centers disability and accessibility (former Seattle Disability Commission chair, universal design agenda) in a way no other candidate does [S1][S3].
- Substantially lower fundraising than Jenks or Kang and no organizational endorsements found, though a similar count of individual contributions [S5][S2].
