## What it does

Proposition No. 1 replaces Seattle's expiring 2019 library levy with a seven-year property tax levy collecting $479,760,000 from 2027 through 2033 to fund Seattle Public Library operating hours and access, collections, technology, maintenance, and administration, as provided in Ordinance 127419 [S1]. The levy adds approximately $0.23 per $1,000 of assessed value, within a maximum total city levy rate of $3.14/$1,000; 2027's levy amount becomes the base for subsequent years through 2033 [S1]. The state senior citizens and disabled persons exemption (RCW 84.36.381) applies [S1]. Passage requires a simple majority (RCW 84.55.050) [S1].

Cost to households: the library estimates about $193/year for the median homeowner [S4]; the pro statement describes the cost as "an additional $9 per month" for the average homeowner [S2]. The pro statement also says the levy provides more than one third of total library funding [S2]; the library states the expiring 2019 levy accounts for about one third of its budget [S4].

## Fiscal mechanics

- Replacement levy (lid lift), not a wholly new tax stream: it replaces the expiring 2019 levy; without voter-approved levies, the city's regular property tax growth is generally capped at 1% per year, and after this levy ends in 2033 the 1% cap would be calculated as if the proposition had not passed [S1].
- Seven-year collection, 2027-2033; rate increase approximately $0.23/$1,000 assessed value [S1].
- Ordinance 127419, Section 6, allocates spending across five categories: operating hours and access, collections, technology, maintenance (including a seismic retrofit of one high-vulnerability landmarked unreinforced-masonry branch), and administration; levy proceeds supplement, not replace, general fund, REET, and other city funding [S3].
- Library breakdown of the $479.7M package: hours and access $229.1M, building maintenance $134.1M, collections $69.4M, technology and online services $38.2M, administration $9M; additions include $28M for seismic retrofits of two branches, $19.2M expanded programming, $7.7M security, $4.1M community resource specialists, and $5M IT infrastructure [S4].
- Accountability: the Library Board adopts an annual operations plan and capital budget, and the Board with the Executive Director/Chief Librarian must submit an annual levy expenditure plan and (2028-2034) an annual progress report to the Mayor and City Council by April 15 each year [S1][S3].

## Arguments for

From the statement in favor (submitted per the pamphlet by Katie B. Wilson, Joy Hollingsworth, and Ken Jennings) [S2]:

- The levy provides more than one third of total library funding, protecting and expanding programs and collections, ensuring skilled staffing, and funding building upgrades for seismic safety, accessibility, and energy efficiency, including at the Central Library [S2].
- "Amid increased federal threats to education and knowledge," Seattle's libraries are described as critical to fighting censorship and misinformation, with a commitment to free and equal access to information [S2].
- The levy expands popular collections, meets rising costs of e-books and materials, and expands learning programs serving all ages, including English learners, job seekers, and people without technology access [S2].
- The cost is described as an additional $9 per month for the average homeowner, with independent oversight and auditing, and senior/disabled tax exemptions; the statement lists endorsements from The Seattle Public Library Foundation, Friends of SPL, AFSCME 2083 Library Employees Union, the Mayor, and Councilmembers [S2].

## Arguments against

No statement against this measure was filed. No one in the jurisdiction contacted King County Elections to serve on the committee to write a statement in opposition [S2].

## Context

- Mayor Katie Wilson's original proposal was $410 million; the City Council added $69.7 million through 11 amendments in committee, bringing the package to $479.7 million, and the council approved it unanimously on April 14, 2026 for the August 4 ballot [S6][S4][S5].
- This levy renews a funding mechanism Seattle voters have repeatedly approved; it replaces the 2019 library levy, and the library says more than 70 percent of the new levy continues hours, collections, and programs from the 2019 levy, including No Late Fines and Peak Picks [S4][S1].
- The library ties the levy to its 2024-2033 Strategic Plan, including maintenance of the now 20-plus-year-old Central Library and continued open hours at 27 locations [S4].
- Local commentary coverage has run in favor of the measure during the campaign period (PubliCola op-ed, July 14, 2026) [S7]; no organized opposition campaign surfaced in the coverage reviewed, consistent with no con committee filing [S2].

## Axis notes

- Tax appetite vs. service level: the choice is between a ~$0.23/$1,000 property tax (≈$193/year median homeowner) and reduced library funding, since the levy covers roughly a third of the library's budget [S1][S4].
- Renewal-plus-growth: this is not a flat renewal; the council expanded the package about 17% above the mayor's proposal, so voters are weighing an enlarged levy, not just continuation [S6].
- Levy reliance as budgeting practice: the measure continues Seattle's pattern of funding core services through voter-approved lid lifts layered over the 1%-capped base levy [S1].
- Absence of organized opposition: no con committee filed, meaning the pamphlet presents only one side; the fiscal trade-off is the main counterweight voters must supply themselves [S2].
