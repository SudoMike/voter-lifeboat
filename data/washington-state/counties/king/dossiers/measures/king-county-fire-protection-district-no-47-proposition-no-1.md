## What it does

- Authorizes King County Fire Protection District No. 47 (headquartered in
  Ravensdale, in rural southeast King County) to restore its regular property
  tax levy to $1.00 per $1,000 of assessed valuation in 2026, for collection in
  2027 [S1][S3].
- The district's levy rate has drifted down to approximately $0.54 per $1,000
  under the statutory 1% annual cap on property tax growth; this measure would
  raise it back by $0.46 per $1,000. The statutory maximum for fire districts
  is $1.50 per $1,000 [S1].
- For the five succeeding years (through 2031), the levy could grow annually by
  the greater of the statutory limit factor (currently 1%) or the 12-month
  CPI-U for the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue area, subject to statutory limits. The
  2031 maximum levy becomes the base for later levy limits [S1].
- The pamphlet does not state a dollar cost for a median household. At the
  stated rates, the increase is $0.46 per $1,000 of assessed value (for
  example, $46 per year per $100,000 of assessed value) [S1].
- The district's board says the restored rate is needed to maintain current
  levels of fire protection, fire suppression, and emergency medical services,
  and to support firefighter safety [S1]. Passage requires a simple majority
  (RCW 84.55.050) [S1].

## Fiscal mechanics

- This is a multi-year levy lid lift of the district's existing regular levy,
  not a new levy or excess levy: it restores the rate to $1.00 per $1,000 (a
  level district voters previously approved in 2008) and then indexes growth
  for five years to the greater of the 1% statutory limit or Seattle-area
  CPI-U, subject to statutory limits [S1].
- The 2031 maximum allowable levy serves as the base for subsequent levy
  limitations, so the lifted level is permanent in the levy base rather than
  expiring back to the old rate [S1].
- No benefit charge is involved; the district is funded through the regular
  property tax levy at issue here [S1].
- The district scheduled a "Levy Lid Lift Community Meeting" for July 18,
  2026, per its website, but the site provides no further published detail on
  the measure [S3].

## Arguments for

- No statement in favor was filed. King County Elections notes that no one in
  the jurisdiction came forward to serve on the pro committee [S2].
- The explanatory statement (the district's official explanation, not an
  advocacy statement) says the Board of Commissioners determined the
  restoration is necessary to maintain current service levels and support
  firefighter safety [S1].

## Arguments against

- No statement against this measure was filed. King County Elections notes
  that no one in the jurisdiction came forward to serve on the con
  committee [S2].

## Context

- District voters previously approved a $1.00 per $1,000 levy rate in 2008;
  the current measure repeats that restoration after 18 years of compression
  under the 1% cap [S1].
- The district serves the Ravensdale area of rural southeast King County and
  lists community outreach on the levy on its website, but no independent news
  coverage of this measure was found in established outlets as of the research
  date [S3].
- The pattern here — a fire district returning to voters because the 1%
  property tax growth cap pushes levy rates down as assessed values rise — is
  the standard mechanism behind Washington fire levy lid lifts, as described
  in the district's own explanatory statement [S1].

## Axis notes

- Tax appetite vs. service level: the core trade-off is an $0.46/$1,000
  property tax increase against the board's stated need to maintain current
  fire and EMS service levels [S1].
- Durability of the increase: CPI-indexed growth for five years plus a
  permanently reset levy base makes this a lasting tax change, not a temporary
  measure [S1].
- Information asymmetry: with neither a pro nor a con committee filed, voters
  have only the district's own explanatory statement to go on; there is no
  organized argument on either side and no press coverage found [S1][S2].
