## What it does

- Asks voters in King County Fire Protection District No. 43 (unincorporated area
  in the Maple Valley/Hobart vicinity) to approve annexing the district into the
  Puget Sound Regional Fire Authority (Puget Sound Fire), under the terms of the
  RFA plan amendment attached to District Resolution No. R-2026-002 [S1].
- The district has a long history of contracting with Puget Sound Fire for fire
  protection and emergency medical services, so annexation would not change who
  delivers services or the level of service residents receive [S1]. Puget Sound
  Fire's own materials list Fire Districts 37 and 43 as part of its current
  service area [S3].
- If approved, annexation takes effect September 30, 2026. The district would
  not dissolve, but it would stop levying its own taxes and benefit charges;
  district property owners would instead pay Puget Sound Fire's taxes and
  benefit charges [S1].
- District voters would gain the right to vote on all future RFA ballot
  measures (including taxes and benefit charges) and would have representation
  on the RFA Governance Board [S1]. The pro committee states annexation
  guarantees two seats on the RFA board [S2].
- The pamphlet does not state a dollar cost to a median household for this
  measure [S1]. Passage requires a simple majority of voters in the area
  proposed for annexation (RCW 52.26.300(4)(a)) [S1].

## Fiscal mechanics

- This is not a new levy or a lid lift; it is a change of taxing jurisdiction.
  After annexation the district would cease levying its own property taxes and
  benefit charges, and property in the district would become subject to Puget
  Sound Fire's levy and its fire benefit charge instead [S1].
- Puget Sound Fire's funding model pairs a property tax levy with a fire
  benefit charge — a fee based on building size and risk rather than assessed
  value alone. In coverage of the parallel Black Diamond annexation vote on the
  same Aug. 4, 2026 ballot, the RFA's structure was described as a property tax
  of $1 per $1,000 of assessed value plus a benefit charge set annually by the
  authority [S4]. (Those figures describe the RFA's general funding structure;
  the pamphlet for this measure does not itemize post-annexation rates for
  District 43 [S1].)
- Annexation would give District 43 voters a vote on future RFA tax and
  benefit-charge measures, which they do not have under the current contract
  arrangement [S1][S2].

## Arguments for

Filed by the pro committee (Andrew Montero, Warren Iverson) [S2]:

- The community has relied on Fire District 43 and Puget Sound Fire for years;
  annexation will not change who responds to 911 calls or the service area.
- The partnership has already delivered benefits, citing the remodel of the
  Maple Heights Fire Station and permitting for the Hobart Station.
- The community currently benefits from the RFA but has no voting
  representation in its decisions; annexation guarantees two seats on the RFA
  board and the right to vote on future RFA ballot measures, including taxes
  and the fire benefit charge.
- The RFA funding model taxes property based on fire risk rather than a single
  standard rate for commercial and residential properties, which the committee
  describes as a more stable and balanced funding structure.

## Arguments against

- No statement against this measure was filed. King County Elections notes
  that no one in the jurisdiction came forward to serve on the con
  committee [S2].

## Context

- Multiple communities in the Puget Sound Fire service area voted on
  annexation-related measures around the same period; the City of Black Diamond
  also has a Puget Sound RFA annexation measure on the Aug. 4, 2026 ballot,
  with coverage there describing the RFA's combined levy-plus-benefit-charge
  cost structure [S4].
- District 43 has contracted with Puget Sound Fire for service for years, so
  this measure formalizes an existing operational relationship rather than
  changing the responding agency [S1][S3].
- No news coverage specific to the District 43 annexation measure itself was
  found in established outlets as of the research date; the measure appears to
  have drawn little independent press attention.

## Axis notes

- Governance vs. status quo: the operative change is representation (voting
  seats on the RFA board and the right to vote on RFA tax measures), not
  service delivery, which stays the same either way [S1][S2].
- Tax-structure preference: annexation shifts residents from district taxation
  to the RFA's levy-plus-benefit-charge model, which allocates cost by building
  size and risk rather than assessed value alone [S1][S4].
- Institutional consolidation vs. independent local district: a yes vote folds
  a small contracting district into a large regional authority for long-term
  stability; a no vote preserves the district's separate taxing authority and
  the existing contract relationship [S1].
- One-sided record: with no con committee filed, the pamphlet presents only
  the case in favor; voters have no organized opposing argument to weigh [S2].
