## What the office does

King County District Court is the county's court of limited jurisdiction.
The work the candidates themselves describe doing there as judges pro tem —
jury trials, arraignments on misdemeanor criminal calendars, traffic and
other infractions, civil protection orders, and small-claims matters — is
the day-to-day docket of the court [S1][S3]. This is a nonpartisan judicial
office elected by district; Position 1 sits in the Northeast Electoral
District, one of seven judicial seats in the court's Northeast Division,
which serves northeast King County from the Redmond courthouse area
[S2]. For most residents who ever encounter a courtroom, a district court
judge is the judge they will actually stand before — in a traffic
infraction, a DUI or other misdemeanor, a protection-order hearing, or a
small-claims dispute [S1][S3].

## Race dynamics

This is an open seat: Judge Marcus Nailor, described by Northwest Asian
Weekly as the Northeast Division's only judge of color, is retiring at the
end of the year [S2]. Three candidates filed, and all three already serve as
judges pro tem in King County District Court itself, so the race is not
about whether anyone can do the job but about which professional formation
voters prefer on this bench [S1][S2][S3].

The backgrounds split cleanly. Jan Trasen spent 26 years as a public
defender and appellate attorney and now judges full-time as an
Administrative Law Judge [S1][S3]. Josh Schaer is an Assistant Attorney
General (government civil/appellate practice, with earlier public defense)
with pro tem service since 2008 and eight years on the Issaquah City
Council [S1][S3]. Bianca Tse spent 16 years as a King County deputy
prosecutor trying cases up to homicide, then built a bilingual private civil
practice, and has sat pro tem since 2021 [S1][S2].

All three self-report "Exceptionally Well Qualified" ratings from multiple
bar associations — Trasen from five unnamed associations, Schaer from KCBA,
Latino/a Bar, QLaw, and the Joint Asian committee, Tse from Loren Miller and
two others [S1][S3]. None of these could be independently verified during
research (KCBA had no public 2026 list at access time); only Tse's Asian Bar
Association rating has third-party press corroboration [S2]. Endorsements
split along adjacent lines: King County Democrats endorsed Trasen [S5],
while LD Democratic organizations divide (1st LD for Schaer; 41st/45th/48th
for Trasen, per candidate statements) and Tse's list leans toward
prosecutors, Eastside municipal officials, and sitting judges [S1].
Fundraising as of mid-July 2026: Tse ~$42k, Trasen ~$28k, Schaer ~$14k [S4].

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate the candidates

- **Professional formation: defense vs. prosecution vs. government civil.**
  Trasen's 26 years in indigent defense and appeals, Tse's 16 years
  prosecuting, and Schaer's AGO civil/appellate career are the clearest
  substantive difference; each shapes courtroom instincts on bail, credibility,
  and sentencing in a misdemeanor court [S1][S2][S3].
- **Depth and type of judicial experience.** Trasen judges full-time (ALJ)
  plus pro tems; Schaer has the longest part-time pro tem tenure (since
  2008); Tse has ~5 years of pro tem service concentrated in this court and
  nearby municipal courts [S1][S2][S3].
- **Approach to therapeutic/community courts.** Trasen explicitly campaigns
  on mental-health and substance-use treatment programs to reduce recidivism;
  Tse and Schaer campaign primarily on readiness, fairness, and experience
  rather than programmatic change [S1][S3].
- **Not differentiating:** all three promise fairness, respect, and
  day-one readiness, and all three claim top bar ratings; partisan
  affiliation is not a valid axis in this nonpartisan judicial race, and no
  candidate has a record of published rulings to compare [S1][S3].
