## Background

Cindy Ryu has been a State Representative for the 32nd District since 2011 and is giving up her House seat to run for the district's Senate seat against fellow Democrat and incumbent Jesse Salomon [S1][S2][S5]. She chairs the House Technology, Economic Development & Veterans Committee and sits on the Appropriations and Consumer Protection & Business committees [S1][S3]; she previously chaired the House Housing Committee for five years [S6]. Before the legislature she was a Shoreline City Councilmember and Mayor (2006–2009), an Allstate insurance agent, and a commercial property manager, with earlier roles at the City of Seattle, University Hospital, and Harborview Medical Center [S1]. She holds an MBA in Operations Management and a BS in Microbiology from the University of Washington [S1]. She told the Washington State Standard she is challenging Salomon because she believes he is "too moderate" for the district [S5][S7].

## Positions

- **Taxes:** Campaigns on "a fairer tax system that asks the wealthiest to pay their share" with relief for working families and small businesses (says she will) [S1][S2]; has backed the capital gains tax and a millionaire's tax (has done, per interview) [S6].
- **Housing:** Wants to increase supply and affordability through mixed-income and social housing, streamlined permitting, and modular construction [S5][S6].
- **Education:** Lists fully funding public education as a top campaign plank [S2].
- **Workers:** Supports minimum wage increases and unions; campaigned on protecting workers from unfair non-compete agreements [S1][S2][S6].
- **Technology/AI:** Supports AI and quantum-computing development paired with regulation, wants a comprehensive state privacy law, opposes subsidies for data centers (though not the facilities themselves), and wants more aggressive legislative action on AI and disaster resilience [S5][S6].
- **Climate:** Backed climate initiatives and wants clean-air legislation targeting working-class communities and communities of color [S6].
- **Broadband:** Cites work bringing broadband access "to every corner of our state" [S1].

## Record

- 16 years (8 terms) in the House; chaired Housing (5 years) and currently chairs Technology, Economic Development & Veterans [S3][S6].
- 2025 session bills signed into law: HB 1006 (consumer protections for service contracts and warranties) and HB 1205 (prohibiting AI-generated deepfake/forged digital likeness content) [S4].
- 2025 session bills passed the Senate: HB 1109 (public facilities district governance), HB 1023 (multistate cosmetology licensure compact), HB 1052 (updating the hate-crime statute so bias need only be a partial motive) [S4].
- Pamphlet cites championing legislation on non-compete agreements, affordable housing for underrepresented communities, and broadband expansion [S1].
- Supported catalytic-converter-theft legislation [S6].
- Criticizes the Senate (and implicitly the incumbent) for stalling bills passed by her House committee [S5].

## Endorsements

Pamphlet lists the 32nd and Snohomish County Democrats, SPEEA, the Washington State Labor Council, the Washington Federation of State Employees, the Washington Education Association, the WA Housing Alliance, the Washington State Nurses Association, and former Rep. Ruth Kagi [S1]. The 32nd District Democrats' 2026 endorsement page lists Ryu as their pick for State Senate [S8]. Kevin Hamel, president of Local 341, endorsed her, citing her record on developmental-disability support [S6].

## Financials

PDC records show 364 contributions totaling about $168,777 for her 2026 Senate campaign (roughly $93,680 from individuals, $75,097 from organizations) [S9]. HeraldNet reported $167,295 raised as of July 8, 2026, slightly ahead of the incumbent [S5].

## Scoring notes

- Longest legislative tenure in the race (House since 2011) but is the challenger for this seat, framing the incumbent as "too moderate" — the race's defining intra-party contrast [S5][S7].
- Distinct issue emphasis on technology policy: AI regulation, state privacy law, and opposition to data-center subsidies, tied to her committee chairmanship [S5][S6].
- Housing approach leans on social housing and mixed-income models, versus the incumbent's supply/permitting and senior-tax-relief emphasis [S5][S6].
- Holds the local party organization's endorsement and major labor endorsements, while the incumbent's endorsements skew toward elected officials [S1][S8].
- Slightly out-fundraised the incumbent as of July 2026 [S5][S9].
