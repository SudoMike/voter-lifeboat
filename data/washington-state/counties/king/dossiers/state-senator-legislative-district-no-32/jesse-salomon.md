## Background

Jesse Salomon is the incumbent State Senator for the 32nd Legislative District, first elected to the Senate in 2018 and serving since 2019 [S1][S3]. He previously served seven years on the Shoreline City Council, including a term as Deputy Mayor [S1][S3]. Before the legislature, he worked as a child welfare prosecutor and as a public defense attorney representing low-income clients [S1][S2]. He holds degrees from Western Washington University and the University of Washington School of Law [S1]. He chairs the Senate Local Government Committee and sits on the Housing, Law & Justice, and Rules committees and the Joint Legislative Audit and Review Committee (JLARC), which he has previously chaired [S1][S3]. He is seeking a third term [S7].

## Positions

- **Taxes:** Supports a "Millionaires Tax" of 9.9% on incomes over $1 million, which his campaign says would cut costs for working families and lower taxes on small businesses (says he will) [S2]. Points to his record of closing corporate tax loopholes and expanding low-income tax rebates (has done, per his statement) [S1].
- **Housing/affordability:** Prioritizes expanding affordable housing supply, lowering housing costs, and raising the income threshold for senior property tax exemptions [S2][S4]. As Local Government chair, says he is working on permitting bottlenecks that raise housing costs [S3].
- **Homelessness:** Emphasizes auditing spending on homelessness programs rather than simply increasing budgets [S4]. Calls for addressing the Aurora Avenue corridor "with compassion and accountability" by connecting people to mental health and addiction treatment [S1].
- **Public safety/justice:** Cites work protecting domestic violence victims on the Law and Justice Committee; pamphlet notes support for gun safety and endorsement by the Alliance for Gun Responsibility and the Snohomish County Sheriff [S1][S2].
- **Federal overreach:** Says he banned ICE face coverings by law and campaigns on "Trump-proofing" the state, citing over 140 laws the legislature has passed to that end [S1][S2].
- **Environment:** Supports environmental restoration and a ban on natural gas fracking; formerly chaired the Agriculture and Natural Resources Committee [S1][S2].
- Describes himself as a "pragmatic progressive" in response to Ryu's criticism that he is too moderate [S7].

## Record

- ESSB 5794 (2025): closed obsolete/special-interest tax loopholes and repealed ineffective tax preferences; passed narrowly over Republican and business opposition [S5][S6].
- ESSB 5611 (2025): cut permitting red tape to speed housing development; passed nearly unanimously [S6].
- ESSB 5202 (2025): updated judicial protection orders for abuse survivors [S6].
- SSB 5157 (2025): allowed DNR to sell habitat restoration materials directly; passed with near-unanimous bipartisan support [S6].
- Pamphlet statement claims: banning ICE face masks, capping rent increases, blocking AI-powered rent-setting schemes, ending sales tax on nonprescription medicines and hygiene products, expanding low-income tax rebates to 460,000 additional households, and cutting small-business taxes [S1].
- Helped pass a $16 billion transportation package his campaign describes as among the state's greenest and most transit-focused [S2].
- 2026-session bill work highlighted on his official page includes law-enforcement identity-concealment restrictions, salmon protection from toxic chemicals, psilocybin therapy access for veterans and first responders, and algorithmic pricing regulation [S3].

## Endorsements

Pamphlet lists: 32nd District Rep. Lauren Davis, Congressman Adam Smith, Washington Senate Democrats, the mayors of Lynnwood and Shoreline, councilmembers from Lynnwood, Shoreline, Seattle, and Edmonds, King and Snohomish County councilmembers, multiple unions, childcare workers, the Alliance for Gun Responsibility, and the Snohomish County Sheriff [S1]. The 32nd District Democrats organization endorsed his opponent Cindy Ryu in this race [see contest file].

## Financials

PDC records show 368 contributions totaling about $155,753 for the 2026 senate campaign (roughly $89,450 from individuals, $66,303 from organizations) [S8]. HeraldNet reported $141,026 raised plus $18,094 carried over as of July 8, 2026 [S4].

## Scoring notes

- Two-term incumbent whose seniority includes a committee chairmanship (Local Government) and a bill-passage record each session, versus a challenger with longer House tenure [S3][S5].
- The core intra-party contrast: Salomon self-describes as a "pragmatic progressive" and stresses auditing homelessness spending and targeted tax-loophole closure, where Ryu positions herself to his left [S4][S7].
- On housing he emphasizes supply, permitting streamlining, and senior property-tax relief rather than the social-housing focus of his main opponent [S2][S4].
- Endorsement base skews toward sitting officeholders (legislators, mayors, sheriff) while the local party organization backed his opponent [S1].
