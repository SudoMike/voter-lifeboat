## Background

Teresa Mosqueda is the incumbent Metropolitan King County Councilmember for District 8, first elected in 2023 and holding the seat since January 2024 [S6][S7]. She is running for re-election in the August 4, 2026 primary; this county race appears in an even year because of a 2022 charter amendment that moved certain county offices to even-year elections beginning in 2026 [S7]. Before joining the County Council she served roughly seven years on the Seattle City Council [S6]. Earlier in her career she spent nearly a decade at the Washington State Labor Council, AFL-CIO, worked at the Children's Alliance (where she led the campaign to implement Apple Health for Kids), and served Latino seniors at Sea Mar Community Health Centers in South Park [S3]. She holds a Master's in Public Administration with a health-policy focus from The Evergreen State College and a BA from the University of Washington [S1]. District 8 covers Burien, White Center, West Seattle, South Park, the Duwamish Valley, Tukwila, and Vashon-Maury Island [S7].

## Positions

- **Housing:** Priorities are expanding affordable and supportive housing and preventing displacement [S1]; her platform groups this as workforce housing, supportive housing, and green housing development [S2].
- **Public safety:** Frames community safety around behavioral health investments and gun-violence prevention rather than enforcement-first approaches [S1]. On sex trafficking along Aurora Avenue N, she told the Seattle Times editorial board she wants to connect trafficked victims to social services, and also cited barriers to massage-parlor licensing (cost, lack of translated materials) as part of the problem — an answer the board criticized as nonresponsive [S6].
- **Working families / labor:** Supports investments in transit, childcare, healthcare, and union job opportunities; describes herself as "unapologetically pro-labor, pro-housing, pro-worker" [S1][S2].
- **Public health:** Emphasizes strengthening public-health infrastructure, behavioral health services, and healthcare access, including for immigrant and refugee communities [S1][S2].
- **Revenue:** Campaign materials tout her record of passing progressive revenue (Seattle's JumpStart payroll tax) as a model, indicating a preference for new progressive revenue over cuts [S2][S7].

## Record

- **King County Council (2024-2025):** Sponsored legislation banning rent price-fixing algorithms, passed September 2025, making King County the 11th U.S. jurisdiction with such a rule [S4]. Passed contracting and procurement legislation strengthening labor standards and equity in county contracts (May 2025) [S4]. Championed housing impact-fee legislation to reduce affordable-housing development barriers (August 2025) [S4]. Co-sponsored updates to the county's Strategic Climate Action Plan (October 2025) [S4]. Proposed a budget amendment responding to federal Continuum of Care funding cuts to permanent housing programs (November 2025) [S4].
- **Regional roles:** Chairs the King County Board of Health, where she led a September 2025 food-safety enforcement rule linking labor-standard violations to health compliance and an October 2025 action affirming healthcare access for immigrants [S4]. Confirmed to the Sound Transit Board in December 2025 [S4].
- **Seattle City Council (2017-2023):** Official biography credits her with doubling investments in affordable housing, expanding protections for domestic workers and hotel workers, and securing paid sick leave for app-based drivers [S3]. Led passage of the JumpStart payroll tax, which generates over $200 million annually for housing, climate, and economic programs [S2][S7].
- **Campaign finance:** PDC records as of 2026-07-16 show approximately $125,433 in itemized contributions across 362 records and approximately $98,143 in expenditures for her 2026 campaign — roughly ten times her nearest opponent's fundraising [S5].

## Endorsements

- Pamphlet statement lists Congresswoman Pramila Jayapal, Governor Bob Ferguson, Attorney General Nick Brown, MLK Labor, SEIU locals, UFCW 3000, Seattle Building Trades, the 34th/33rd/11th District Democrats, Progreso, Latino Progress, Washington Conservation Action, WA Housing Alliance Action Fund, NW Women's Political Caucus, "and 200+ more" (self-reported) [S1].
- Campaign website adds Seattle City Attorney Erika Evans, UFW President Teresa Romero, SEIU 6, SEIU 925, and PROTEC17 [S2].
- News coverage of her launch confirmed backing from Ferguson, Brown, Jayapal, and roughly a dozen labor unions [S7].
- The Seattle Times editorial board endorsed her opponent Nick Duda in the 2026 primary [S6].

## Scoring notes

- Only candidate in the race with an elected record: two Seattle City Council terms plus a King County Council term, with specific passed legislation (rent-algorithm ban, procurement/labor standards, JumpStart tax) [S3][S4][S6].
- Represents the labor-progressive lane: institutional Democratic, union, and advocacy-group endorsements are consolidated behind her [S1][S2][S7].
- Approaches public safety and homelessness primarily through behavioral health, services, and prevention, in contrast to her main opponent's enforcement-plus-accountability framing [S1][S6].
- Largest fundraising operation in the race by roughly an order of magnitude [S5].
- The region's largest newspaper editorial board endorsed her challenger, criticizing one of her public-safety answers as nonresponsive — the main countervailing signal against an otherwise broad endorsement sweep [S6].
