## What the office does
A Washington State Representative is one of two House members (plus one Senator) elected from each legislative district to the 98-member State House. Representatives serve two-year terms in a part-time citizen legislature (105-day sessions in odd years, 60-day in even years). They write and vote on state law and the operating, capital, and transportation budgets — controlling K-12 funding, the state tax structure (including the newly enacted "millionaire earner" income tax), housing and land-use mandates on cities, behavioral-health funding, transportation projects such as SR 18, and policing/criminal-justice statutes. This is the August 4, 2026 top-two primary: the two highest vote-getters advance regardless of party.

## District and race dynamics
Legislative District 5 covers eastern King County suburbs and exurbs including parts of Issaquah, Snoqualmie, Black Diamond, and Covington. Democrats currently hold all three of the district's legislative seats, but regional press describes it as territory "Republicans have eyed as [a place] where they might make gains" — one of the more genuinely contestable King County districts.

The seat's recent history shapes the race: after Sen. Bill Ramos died in 2025, Rep. Victoria Hunt was appointed to the Senate and the King County Council appointed Issaquah City Councilmember Zach Hall (D) to her House seat in June 2025. Hall, now seeking election in his own right, passed two bipartisan bills in his first session, has the field's largest war chest (~$44k) and broadest endorsement slate (Gov. Ferguson, Rep. Schrier, labor, firefighters). Republican Michelle Bennett, Mercer Island's police chief with a 35-year law-enforcement career largely inside this district, is a credible challenger (~$21k, 81 contributions) running on tax restraint and public safety. Democrat Aimee Warmerdam (DSA and Whole Washington organizer, ~$3.7k) challenges Hall from the left with a single-payer/social-housing program. Topher Leritz runs a no-party, no-donations, no-endorsements campaign. The most likely primary question is whether Bennett consolidates the right while Hall and Warmerdam split Democratic votes — with the Hall–Bennett tax and public-safety contrast the expected general-election frame.

## Differentiating issue axes
1. **Income tax / tax levels:** The sharpest documented contrast — Hall voted for the new millionaire-earner income tax; Bennett opposes an income tax and calls the recent budget "the largest tax increase in state history"; Warmerdam wants substantially more progressive taxation (millionaire's tax, large-employer payroll tax); Leritz emphasizes spending accountability.
2. **Public safety:** Bennett runs on restoring "clear tools" for police, backed by three decades as a chief; Hall emphasizes first-responder support via his fire-board background; Warmerdam does not center policing; Leritz pairs safety with mental-health services.
3. **Scale of economic ambition (D vs. D):** Hall is an incremental consensus-builder (energy-cost bills, housing reforms); Warmerdam proposes structural change — single-payer healthcare, $26 minimum wage, 32-hour workweek, social housing, state bank.
4. **Experience type:** Legislative/municipal (Hall), law-enforcement executive plus education doctorate (Bennett), grassroots organizing (Warmerdam), private-sector/nonpartisan outsider (Leritz).
5. **Money and interest-group politics:** Hall accepts broad institutional support; Warmerdam refuses corporate PAC money; Leritz refuses all donations and endorsements — a clean spectrum on campaign-finance practice.
