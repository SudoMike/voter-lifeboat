## What this office does

A U.S. Representative serves a two-year term in the House of Representatives: writing and voting on federal law, appropriating federal spending (including the defense budget), declaring war and overseeing the executive branch, and providing constituent services (casework with federal agencies). Members exercise outsized influence through committee roles — relevant here because the incumbent is the top Democrat on the House Armed Services Committee, which drafts the annual National Defense Authorization Act.

## The district and the field

WA-9 covers parts of Seattle and Bellevue and the south King County communities of Renton, Tukwila, Kent, Des Moines, SeaTac, and Federal Way [S2]; it is one of the state's most diverse districts (roughly 24% Asian, 15% Hispanic/Latino, 12% Black per 2024 coverage) [S4]. Five candidates were certified to the August 4, 2026 top-two primary: incumbent Adam Smith (D), Melissa Chaudhry (D), Kshama Sawant (Independent Party), Doug Basler (R), and Jacob Perasso (Socialist Workers Party) [S1]. The top two vote-getters, regardless of party, advance to the November 3 general election.

## Race dynamics

- Smith, first elected in 1996, is the dean of the state's U.S. House delegation. He won 2024 with 65.44% against Chaudhry [S3].
- The race's defining feature is a crowded left flank: Sawant (ex-Seattle councilmember, Stranger-endorsed), Chaudhry (2024 runner-up), and Perasso all challenge Smith from the left, primarily over U.S. support for Israel, war spending, and corporate/PAC money [S2][S3][S4].
- NPI analysis notes the left-flank split could allow Basler, the lone Republican and a five-time Smith challenger, to take the second general-election slot [S3].
- The Stranger's endorsement of Sawant drew public criticism (one strategist called it "a total embarrassment"), and its write-up of Chaudhry — reporting she would switch to the Green Party if elected and omitted LGBTQ+ protections from her platform strategically — prompted the Washington State Stonewall Democrats to urge organizations to reconsider Chaudhry endorsements [S3].
- Money (FEC, 2026 cycle): Smith ~$1.13M raised (including ~$420K from PACs), $847K cash on hand (3/31); Sawant ~$609K raised, essentially all individual donations (6/30); Chaudhry ~$3.5K in new receipts (3/31); Basler $0 [S5].

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate these candidates

1. **U.S. military aid to Israel / defense budget.** The clearest divider. Smith (HASC ranking member, NDAA author, AIPAC/defense-industry donations) vs. Sawant (arms embargo as first priority) and Chaudhry ("stop funding Israel," audit the Pentagon). Perasso opposes U.S. Middle East wars but explicitly defends Israel's right to exist as a refuge for Jews. Basler does not campaign on this axis.
2. **Insider vs. outsider / party system.** Smith runs on incumbency and establishment endorsements; Chaudhry runs as a Democrat (with a reported post-election Green switch); Sawant runs explicitly to build a new workers' party; Perasso runs to build a revolutionary party; Basler is a longtime local GOP officer.
3. **Campaign money.** Smith accepts PAC/AIPAC money; Sawant and Chaudhry refuse corporate PAC money and make that refusal central; Basler and Perasso have effectively no fundraising.
4. **Healthcare.** All three left candidates back universal/single-payer care but differ in mechanism and urgency: Smith wants a "path to" single-payer; Sawant wants free healthcare now funded by taxing the rich; Chaudhry wants guaranteed universal care plus wealth taxes. Basler does not campaign on universal coverage.
5. **Economic policy and cost of living.** Sawant: national rent control, $25 minimum wage; Chaudhry: $25 minimum wage, billionaire wealth tax, public jobs guarantee; Smith: build more housing, workforce development, middle-out growth; Basler: attributes affordability problems to state climate policy (Climate Commitment Act), gas taxes, and regulation — the only candidate on this side of the axis.
6. **Immigration/ICE.** Sawant ("shut down ICE") and Chaudhry ("abolish ICE," her husband's detention as signature story) go further than Smith; Perasso calls for immigrant amnesty; Basler emphasizes public safety instead.
7. **Record vs. promise.** Only Smith (29 years of federal votes/bills) and Sawant (a decade of enacted Seattle ordinances) have legislative records; Chaudhry offers litigation and nonprofit work; Basler and Perasso have no governing record.
