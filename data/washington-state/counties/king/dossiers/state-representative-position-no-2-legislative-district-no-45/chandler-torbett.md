## Background

Chandler Torbett is a technology attorney and corporate counsel advising on regulatory, privacy, and business issues, and a former general counsel for a regional healthcare system and small business owner; he has never held elected office [S1]. NPI reports he moved to Washington after accepting a legal role with Amazon [S4]; the Seattle Times reports he moved to Washington in 2021 [S5]. He was born and raised on the Muscogee Nation reservation in Oklahoma and says his tribe helped put him through law school and helped him purchase a home, an experience he credits for his belief "in the power of government to solve people's problems" [S4][S5]. He holds a Juris Doctor and a BA in Political Science [S1]. He is a Redmond father of three, a member of the Redmond Rotary Club, a Little League coach, a pro bono volunteer for families experiencing homelessness and immigration cases, and a volunteer contributor to the UW Racial Restrictive Covenants Project [S1][S5].

## Positions

- **Affordability (central theme):** Frames affordability broadly — "it's housing, it's childcare, it's groceries, it's insurance premiums"; pledges urgency on lowering housing and childcare costs and supporting small businesses [S1][S4].
- **Housing:** Supports increasing supply through zoning reform and denser development near transit ("I'm a proponent of density where it makes sense — especially around transit"); says people who work in Redmond should be able to live in Redmond; explicitly does not call himself an "urbanist" [S4].
- **Taxes:** Supports progressive revenue including the million-dollar earners income tax and defends the capital gains tax; says lower-income residents paying a higher share of income in taxes is "a problem"; echoed Seattle Times concerns that the 2026 income tax lacked accompanying broad-based tax relief [S4][S5].
- **Childcare/education:** "A huge proponent of universal pre-K" — "If Oklahoma can do it, surely Washington can" [S4].
- **AI / technology:** Calls his district "ground zero for AI"; wants consumer-protection guardrails on AI in hiring, healthcare, and financial decisions while encouraging innovation and jobs; presents himself as the candidate prepared for this issue [S1][S4][S5].
- **Climate:** Wants to accelerate the clean-energy transition primarily by removing administrative and permitting obstacles that slow infrastructure projects [S4].
- **Government accountability:** Says Washington suffers a "crisis of faith in government" and that the Legislature must prove state programs deliver results — "We've got to prove we can use this money wisely"; open to market-based solutions over government intervention in some cases [S4][S5].
- **Federal issues:** Pledges to "push back on Trump" [S1].

## Endorsements

- Seattle Times editorial board (primary endorsement: voters should embrace "his solutions-oriented campaign and give his drive for greater accountability a chance") [S5].
- Retiring incumbent Rep. Larry Springer (who endorsed both Torbett and Kritzer) [S1][S2][S5].
- State Sen. Claudia Kauffman; Run for Something; Washington Federation of State Employees (WFSE), per his campaign website [S2].

## Scoring notes

- Positions himself on execution and accountability rather than ideology in what he calls a "battle of progressives" with little policy contrast — "What I think I bring that's unique is the delivery and execution piece" [S4].
- The only candidate in the race centering AI regulation and tech-sector expertise, which he ties to the district's economy [S1][S4][S5].
- First-time candidate without a governing record, versus an opponent with six-plus years on a city council; the Seattle Times called him "admittedly the dark horse" but endorsed him as the better fit for the retiring pragmatist Springer's seat [S4][S5].
- Willing to break with party orthodoxy (market-based solutions, criticism of tax packages lacking relief for ordinary households), which the Seattle Times contrasted favorably against Kritzer [S5].
- Trails in fundraising (~$34K across 68 reported contributions vs ~$109K for Kritzer, per PDC data) and in institutional endorsements [S3].
