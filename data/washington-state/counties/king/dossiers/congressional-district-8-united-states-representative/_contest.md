## What this office does

A U.S. Representative serves a two-year term in the U.S. House of Representatives, one of 435 voting members. The job is to write, sponsor, and vote on federal legislation (taxes, healthcare, immigration, defense, farm policy), conduct oversight of the executive branch through committees, direct federal appropriations — including "Community Project" earmarks back to the district — and provide constituent services (help with federal agencies such as the VA, IRS, and Social Security). House members also vote on impeachment and, with the Senate, control the federal budget.

## This race

- **Format:** August 4, 2026 top-two primary; the two highest vote-getters advance to the November 3 general election regardless of party.
- **The district:** WA-8 is the only Washington district that spans the Cascades, combining suburban/exurban King and Pierce County communities (Issaquah, Auburn, Graham) with rural Central Washington (Wenatchee, Leavenworth, Ellensburg) and a slice of Snohomish County (Darrington). Ballotpedia lists its Cook Partisan Voting Index as D+3 — Democratic-leaning but historically competitive; it elected only Republicans until Schrier flipped it in 2018.
- **The field:** Six candidates — incumbent Democrat Kim Schrier seeking a fifth term; four Republicans (Trinh Ha, Spencer Meline, Bob Hagglund, Andres Valleza); and perennial Democratic-preference candidate Keith Arnold.
- **Dynamics:** Schrier holds an overwhelming resource advantage (roughly $2.45M raised and $3.29M cash on hand per FEC filings, versus ~$126K for Meline, ~$103K for Ha, ~$18K for Valleza, and no reported totals for Hagglund or Arnold). The four Republicans are effectively competing with each other for the second general-election slot, and county GOP organizations have split: King County GOP endorsed Meline, Douglas County GOP endorsed Hagglund, and both Meline and Hagglund claim the Snohomish County GOP. No prominent challenge to Schrier from the left appeared this cycle.

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate these candidates

1. **Incumbency and federal record vs. outsider change.** Schrier runs on four terms of committee work, earmarked district projects, and healthcare legislation; every challenger runs against "career politicians," though from different angles (Ha: federal-waste insider; Meline: small-business owner; Hagglund: private-sector problem-solver; Valleza: frontline public servant; Arnold: anti-system reformer).
2. **Healthcare policy.** Schrier (a pediatrician) centers drug-price caps, Medicare negotiation, and insurance regulation and attacks Republican healthcare cuts; the Republican field mentions healthcare costs mainly as a cost-of-living item without detailed counter-proposals.
3. **Immigration and border security.** All four Republicans call for a secure border (Hagglund and Meline pair it with protecting agricultural labor); Schrier lists immigration as an issue but does not center enforcement; Arnold would focus enforcement on abusive employers.
4. **Public safety.** Valleza is the most distinct — federal prosecution of gang/gun crime from a corrections background; Hagglund opposes lenient prosecutors; Schrier points to funding body cameras and rapid DNA testing for law enforcement.
5. **Taxes and spending.** Republicans want permanent tax relief (Valleza would also eliminate capital gains taxes; Valleza and Ha stress audits/waste); Schrier and Arnold favor progressive taxation, with Schrier targeting corporate price gouging.
6. **Democracy/process reform.** Unique to Arnold: ranked-choice voting and judicial-review theories dominate his platform. Ha has signed a term-limits pledge.

Note: within the Republican field itself, policy differences are modest; differentiation is mostly biography (refugee/federal consultant vs. restaurateur vs. data scientist/party chair vs. parole officer), geography (Meline is the main east-of-Cascades resident candidate), organizational backing, and fundraising.
