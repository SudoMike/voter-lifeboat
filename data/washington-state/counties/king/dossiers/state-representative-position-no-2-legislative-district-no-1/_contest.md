## What the office does
A Washington State Representative is one of two House members (plus one Senator) elected from each legislative district to the 98-member State House. Representatives serve two-year terms in a part-time citizen legislature that convenes in Olympia (105-day sessions in odd years, 60-day in even years). They write and vote on state law and the state operating, capital, and transportation budgets — controlling K-12 school funding, state tax structure (sales, B&O, property-tax caps, the capital gains tax), housing and land-use mandates on cities (e.g., Growth Management Act, middle-housing laws), Medicaid and behavioral-health funding, and policing/criminal-justice statutes. This is the August 4, 2026 top-two primary: the two highest vote-getters advance to the general election regardless of party.

## District and race dynamics
Legislative District 1 spans the King/Snohomish county line, covering Bothell, Kenmore, Woodinville, Lake Forest Park, and nearby areas. The seat is held by Democrat Shelley Kloba, in office since 2017.

The distinctive dynamic is an intra-party challenge: Bothell Deputy Mayor Jenne Alderks (D) is running against the incumbent and states she won the 1st LD Democrats' sole endorsement, while Kloba retains a long list of labor, environmental, and elected-official endorsements (including the House Speaker) and out-raises her roughly 3.5:1 in PDC-reported contributions ($38.5k vs $11.5k as of mid-July 2026). Libertarian Jeff Lyon (a Navy veteran and tech-security executive, ~$59 raised) and Republican Cliff Moon (construction-trades veteran endorsed by the King County GOP, no PDC-reported fundraising) contest the right-of-center lane in a district that elects Democrats. With two low-resource right-of-center candidates splitting that vote, a plausible outcome is a Democrat-vs-Democrat general election, making the Kloba–Alderks contrast the core question for most voters.

## Differentiating issue axes
1. **Incumbency vs. fresh local voice (D vs. D):** Kloba's nine-year legislative record — data privacy, AI/consumer protection, gambling and cannabis regulation — against Alderks's municipal-executive record (crisis response, community court, housing) and human-services career. Their policy platforms overlap heavily; the real axis is experience type and the local party's endorsement split.
2. **Tax structure:** Kloba wants to make the tax code less regressive (closing loopholes, no elimination promise); Alderks emphasizes affordability and universal healthcare; Lyon would eliminate state taxes for most households under $125k and require voter approval for any income tax; Moon centers property-tax relief.
3. **State vs. local control of housing/land use:** Lyon and Moon would roll back state zoning mandates and GMA requirements in favor of city control; Kloba and Alderks back state-level housing-supply and affordability action.
4. **Public safety approach:** Moon runs on fully funding police and accountability for offenders; Lyon mixes law-enforcement credentials with decriminalizing "victimless" conduct; Alderks emphasizes crisis-response and community-court alternatives; Kloba emphasizes behavioral-health access.
5. **Health care ambition:** Alderks explicitly backs universal healthcare coverage; Kloba emphasizes behavioral-health access within existing structures.
