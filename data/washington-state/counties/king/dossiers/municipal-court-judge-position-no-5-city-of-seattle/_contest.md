## What the office does

Seattle Municipal Court is the City of Seattle's court of limited
jurisdiction: it handles misdemeanors and gross misdemeanors charged under
city ordinances (theft, DUI, assault, domestic-violence misdemeanors),
traffic and parking infractions, and other municipal violations, and runs
specialty calendars such as Veterans Treatment Court [S2]. Candidates in
this race describe the docket the same way — criminal calendars, jail
calendars, vehicle-impound hearings — and repeatedly note that municipal
court is many residents' first and often only contact with the justice
system [S1][S3]. Judges are elected citywide to nonpartisan positions.
The practical stakes are how a judge handles bail and release, diversion
and treatment alternatives, language access, and day-to-day courtroom
dignity — not policy-making [S1][S3].

## Race dynamics

Position 5 is effectively an open seat. Incumbent Judge Willie Gregory
(first elected in 2011, current term through January 2027, presiding over
Veterans Treatment Court) is not seeking another term, and per Garmon
Newsom's pamphlet statement has endorsed Newsom "to succeed him" [S1][S2].
Three candidates filed, but Lindsay Calkins — an appellate attorney and the
only candidate without pro tem judicial experience — announced her
withdrawal on her campaign site, citing family reasons, after the pamphlet
was printed; her statement still appears in the pamphlet [S1][S3].

That leaves a two-way contest with a clean professional split. Garmon
Newsom II is a 30-year prosecutor and municipal-government lawyer (Bronx
DA, NYC Special Narcotics, Renton Chief Prosecutor, Burien City Attorney)
now sitting pro tem at Seattle Municipal Court [S1][S3]. Gabe Rothstein is
a 20-year public defender (70+ trials to verdict, WSBA Character and
Fitness Board chair) sitting pro tem since 2024 in six regional courts
[S1][S3]. Endorsement blocs mirror the split: Newsom has the Seattle Times
editorial board and the retiring incumbent [S1][S4]; Rothstein has the King
County Democrats, two LD Democratic organizations, and the SEIU 925 public
defense chapter [S1][S5]. Both claim substantial judicial endorsement lists
[S1][S3]. Fundraising as of mid-July 2026: Newsom ~$77k, Rothstein ~$35k,
Calkins ~$8k (closed out) [S6].

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate the candidates

- **Prosecution vs. public defense.** The defining difference: Newsom's
  career prosecuting and representing city governments vs. Rothstein's
  career defending indigent clients. In a court whose entire criminal docket
  is misdemeanors, this shapes instincts on charging credibility, release,
  and sentencing [S1][S3].
- **Bail/release policy explicitness.** Rothstein states release decisions
  should turn on flight risk and public safety rather than bail
  affordability; Newsom does not stake out a comparably specific position
  [S3].
- **Diversion framing.** Both support treatment and diversion; Newsom
  stresses that diversion must be "evidence-based, effective, and
  accountable" and centers restorative justice with community partners;
  Rothstein centers treatment-over-punishment and second chances from his
  drug-court and recovery-board experience [S1][S3].
- **Judicial experience depth.** Newsom's pro tem service is in Seattle
  Municipal Court itself with decades of courtroom practice behind it;
  Rothstein's pro tem service is newer (2024) but spans six courts and
  includes the jail and impound calendars this court runs [S1][S3].
- **Not differentiating:** both self-report "Exceptionally Well Qualified"
  ratings from minority bar associations (neither set independently
  verified), both emphasize dignity and being heard, and partisan inference
  is inappropriate for this nonpartisan judicial office [S1][S3].
