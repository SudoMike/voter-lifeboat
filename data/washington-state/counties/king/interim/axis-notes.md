## assessor  (depth: deep)
- **Insider continuity vs. outside reform.** Dams argues the office's
  professional staff are sound and experience prevents costly mistakes;
  Roberts argues the failure is at the leadership level and calls for a
  citizen oversight committee; Foxcurran argues the system structurally
  favors large corporate appellants; Scarimbolo argues the valuation system
  itself is non-uniform and unlawful [S4]. Where a candidate locates the
  problem (leadership, structure, or the system itself) is the clearest
  dividing line.
- **Direct valuation expertise.** Foxcurran (certified appraiser) and Dams
  (managing the appraisal operation) have hands-on assessment credentials;
  Roberts offers elected-executive and budget experience plus IAAO
  membership; Scarimbolo offers taxpayer-side appeal experience [S4].
- **Legislative agenda in Olympia.** Foxcurran would push to lift the 1%
  levy-growth cap, add homestead protections, and create a renter's tax
  credit; Roberts and Dams emphasize expanding existing exemption programs;
  Scarimbolo emphasizes audits and uniformity enforcement rather than new
  relief programs [S4]. Note the Assessor can advocate for but not enact
  any of these.
- **Equity framing of the tax burden.** Foxcurran centers a
  corporate-vs-working-people burden-shift argument; Scarimbolo centers a
  taxpayer-rights/due-process argument; Dams centers administrative
  accuracy; Roberts centers customer service, backlog reduction, and tribal
  liaison/oversight structures [S4].
- **Not differentiating:** all four candidates say property-tax pressure on
  homeowners is too high and all promise transparency and better customer
  service; those themes do not distinguish anyone in this field [S4].

## congressional-district-1-united-states-representative  (depth: deep)
1. **Incumbency and record vs. outsider status.** DelBene runs on a
   13-year legislative record (Child Tax Credit expansion, housing supply
   legislation, trade/tariff bills). Every other candidate runs explicitly
   against career politicians; Etzkorn (school board) is the only
   challenger with any elected experience.
2. **Health care system design.** Medicare for All (Gordon, Nickel,
   Hildebrand, Silva-adjacent no — Silva emphasizes "health freedom" and
   anti-monopoly instead) vs. strengthening the ACA/Medicaid and lowering
   premiums within the current system (DelBene) vs. pilot-tested
   incrementalism before any national single-payer rollout (Kincaid).
3. **Economic left-right span within the Democratic field.** Gordon,
   Nickel, and Hildebrand propose wealth taxes, national rent
   stabilization/control, UBI or jobs guarantees, and (Nickel)
   nationalizing AI/automation; Kincaid pairs a large working-class income
   tax cut with "socialism doesn't work" and social positions to the
   incumbent's right; DelBene favors targeted tax credits and bipartisan
   housing-supply incentives.
4. **Party system itself.** Etzkorn's central pitch is anti-two-party
   structural reform (term limits, dark-money bans, insider-trading bans);
   Hildebrand and Gordon share the anti-corporate-money theme but within
   the Democratic party; DelBene chairs a national party committee.
5. **Tech and AI.** Gordon would halt data-center construction and
   regulate AI; Nickel would nationalize AI; Kincaid treats AI-era energy
   demand as a housing/energy manufacturing opportunity; Silva emphasizes
   cybersecurity investment; DelBene (former Microsoft executive) sponsors
   data-privacy legislation.
6. **Israel/foreign policy.** Gordon would end all U.S. aid to Israel and
   refuses AIPAC money (as does Kincaid refuse corporate PAC money);
   Kincaid supports Ukraine and "zero tolerance for antisemitism";
   DelBene's pamphlet focuses on domestic policy.

## congressional-district-7-united-states-representative  (depth: deep)
1. **Incumbency and record vs. outsider status.** Jayapal is the only candidate with any legislative record (nine years in the House, committee and Progressive Caucus leadership, 193 sponsored bills). All three challengers have never held elected office.
2. **Direction of challenge: from the left vs. from the right.** Kirkland runs explicitly to Jayapal's left (publicly owned utilities and grocery stores, hostility to the Democratic establishment); Sheth runs from the right (law and order, funding police, fiscal restraint, energy independence). This left-right axis is the clearest substantive spread in the race.
3. **Public safety and policing.** Sheth (ex-Marine, ex-Lakewood police officer) makes restoring "law & order" in Seattle his lead theme; Jayapal's immigration-enforcement bills (e.g., ICE Out of Our Faces Act) point the opposite direction on federal enforcement posture. Kirkland's platform does not address policing.
4. **Healthcare and economic program.** Jayapal and Kirkland both back universal healthcare and free college; they differ on degree (Kirkland adds public ownership of utilities/groceries) and on vehicle (Jayapal has actual bills). Sheth does not campaign on healthcare.
5. **Immigration.** Jayapal (OneAmerica founder, Immigration Subcommittee ranking member) campaigns against ICE/CBP enforcement actions; Sheth, himself an immigrant, promotes legal-pathway immigration and has reportedly posted content opposing birthright citizenship. Notably, three of the four candidates are immigrants or children of immigrants from India.
6. **Israel/Middle East policy.** Jayapal authored an Iran War Powers resolution and is generally pro-Palestinian (though she voted for Iron Dome funding, per Daily UW); Blomstrom's position (severing ties with and derecognizing Israel) is embedded in an antisemitic platform and sits outside normal policy debate. This axis mostly distinguishes Blomstrom's fringe candidacy rather than offering voters a genuine policy choice.

Axes that do NOT differentiate: climate rhetoric (Jayapal, Blomstrom, and to a lesser degree Sheth all invoke environment/energy but only Jayapal has legislation); "anti-establishment/anti-corruption" framing, which every challenger uses in some form.

## congressional-district-8-united-states-representative  (depth: deep)
1. **Incumbency and federal record vs. outsider change.** Schrier runs on four terms of committee work, earmarked district projects, and healthcare legislation; every challenger runs against "career politicians," though from different angles (Ha: federal-waste insider; Meline: small-business owner; Hagglund: private-sector problem-solver; Valleza: frontline public servant; Arnold: anti-system reformer).
2. **Healthcare policy.** Schrier (a pediatrician) centers drug-price caps, Medicare negotiation, and insurance regulation and attacks Republican healthcare cuts; the Republican field mentions healthcare costs mainly as a cost-of-living item without detailed counter-proposals.
3. **Immigration and border security.** All four Republicans call for a secure border (Hagglund and Meline pair it with protecting agricultural labor); Schrier lists immigration as an issue but does not center enforcement; Arnold would focus enforcement on abusive employers.
4. **Public safety.** Valleza is the most distinct — federal prosecution of gang/gun crime from a corrections background; Hagglund opposes lenient prosecutors; Schrier points to funding body cameras and rapid DNA testing for law enforcement.
5. **Taxes and spending.** Republicans want permanent tax relief (Valleza would also eliminate capital gains taxes; Valleza and Ha stress audits/waste); Schrier and Arnold favor progressive taxation, with Schrier targeting corporate price gouging.
6. **Democracy/process reform.** Unique to Arnold: ranked-choice voting and judicial-review theories dominate his platform. Ha has signed a term-limits pledge.

Note: within the Republican field itself, policy differences are modest; differentiation is mostly biography (refugee/federal consultant vs. restaurateur vs. data scientist/party chair vs. parole officer), geography (Meline is the main east-of-Cascades resident candidate), organizational backing, and fundraising.

## congressional-district-9-united-states-representative  (depth: deep)
1. **U.S. military aid to Israel / defense budget.** The clearest divider. Smith (HASC ranking member, NDAA author, AIPAC/defense-industry donations) vs. Sawant (arms embargo as first priority) and Chaudhry ("stop funding Israel," audit the Pentagon). Perasso opposes U.S. Middle East wars but explicitly defends Israel's right to exist as a refuge for Jews. Basler does not campaign on this axis.
2. **Insider vs. outsider / party system.** Smith runs on incumbency and establishment endorsements; Chaudhry runs as a Democrat (with a reported post-election Green switch); Sawant runs explicitly to build a new workers' party; Perasso runs to build a revolutionary party; Basler is a longtime local GOP officer.
3. **Campaign money.** Smith accepts PAC/AIPAC money; Sawant and Chaudhry refuse corporate PAC money and make that refusal central; Basler and Perasso have effectively no fundraising.
4. **Healthcare.** All three left candidates back universal/single-payer care but differ in mechanism and urgency: Smith wants a "path to" single-payer; Sawant wants free healthcare now funded by taxing the rich; Chaudhry wants guaranteed universal care plus wealth taxes. Basler does not campaign on universal coverage.
5. **Economic policy and cost of living.** Sawant: national rent control, $25 minimum wage; Chaudhry: $25 minimum wage, billionaire wealth tax, public jobs guarantee; Smith: build more housing, workforce development, middle-out growth; Basler: attributes affordability problems to state climate policy (Climate Commitment Act), gas taxes, and regulation — the only candidate on this side of the axis.
6. **Immigration/ICE.** Sawant ("shut down ICE") and Chaudhry ("abolish ICE," her husband's detention as signature story) go further than Smith; Perasso calls for immigrant amnesty; Basler emphasizes public safety instead.
7. **Record vs. promise.** Only Smith (29 years of federal votes/bills) and Sawant (a decade of enacted Seattle ordinances) have legislative records; Chaudhry offers litigation and nonprofit work; Basler and Perasso have no governing record.

## council-district-no-2-metropolitan-king-county  (depth: deep)
1. **How to fund social housing:** Hasegawa backs a county property-tax
   levy for social housing now ("act with urgency"); Saldaña is reticent
   about new property taxes, preferring progressive revenue options and
   state-level action. This was the sharpest disagreement at the June 2026
   forum [C1].
2. **Experience type:** legislative record (Saldaña: HEAL Act, Voting
   Rights Act, Domestic Workers Bill of Rights, Labor Committee chair) vs.
   executive/project delivery (Hasegawa: Port terminal expansion, Port
   childcare center, state agency director) vs. internal county
   operations knowledge (Mboya: seven years in county budgeting and
   contracting) [C1][C5].
3. **Transit trade-offs:** Hasegawa explicitly prioritizes preserving bus
   service over fleet electrification if forced to choose and wants to
   accelerate the RapidRide R Line; Saldaña supports a countywide transit
   levy and "free" transit ambitions but was less definitive on the
   service-vs-electrification trade [C1][C2].
4. **Insider vs. first-timer:** Mboya is the only candidate without
   elected experience, running on lived experience (homelessness, foster
   care, immigrant background) and county-staff perspective; she has not
   articulated specific revenue positions, distinguishing her from both
   frontrunners' tax-the-wealthy platforms [C5].
5. **Coalition base:** county Democratic Party + service unions (Saldaña)
   vs. governors/executive + maritime and trades unions (Hasegawa) — a
   proxy for which wing of the region's labor-progressive coalition each
   answers to [C1][C2][C5].

## council-district-no-5-city-of-seattle  (depth: deep)
1. **Police staffing and enforcement vs. non-police response.** Kang wants to rebuild SPD staffing and add a second D5 precinct, shut down open-air drug markets, and (per her site) use the Aurora SOAP zone; Jenks emphasizes voluntary treatment, housing-first, and community responders and opposes shelter conditioned on treatment; James goes furthest — CARE team with independent dispatch, restorative justice, poverty framed as the root safety issue; Georgakopoulos wants streets kept "free of drug abusers, homeless encampments and violent offenders" [C2][C4].
2. **Surveillance technology.** James opposes ALPRs, CCTV, and Axon contracts outright; Jenks opposes surveillance expansion; Kang questions its effectiveness and stresses privacy/civil-rights balance; Georgakopoulos has no stated position [C2][C3][C4].
3. **Housing strategy.** Jenks: 1,000 affordable units in-district plus rent stabilization, just-cause eviction, right to counsel. James: social housing at 30-40% of stock, vacancy tax, tenant unions, anti-sweep. Kang: permitting reform, middle housing/TOD, shifting affordable-housing funding to payroll-tax revenue, infrastructure concurrency. Georgakopoulos: critiques concentration of low-income housing in D5 without an affirmative build platform [C2][C4][C6].
4. **Taxes and revenue.** James proposes new corporate taxes, a vacancy tax, and guaranteed basic income; Jenks's platform is spending-oriented but not tax-centered; Kang pairs cuts with selective new taxes; Georgakopoulos is dismissive of "tax the rich" framing [C2][C4][C6].
5. **Candidate profile / experience type.** Organizer-coalition builder (Jenks), educator-banker technocrat (Kang), systems-navigator advocate (James), small-business owner (Georgakopoulos) — relevant because none has held elected office, so voters are choosing among professional backgrounds rather than records [C2][C3][C4][C6].

Axes that do NOT differentiate the top three: all three support sanctuary-city/immigrant protections, more affordable housing in some form, small-business support (storefront/vendor programs), and safety improvements on Aurora and Lake City Way — differences are of degree and mechanism, not direction [C4][C6]. Georgakopoulos is the outlier on most of these.

## council-district-no-8-metropolitan-king-county  (depth: deep)
1. **Experience vs. outsider change:** An incumbent with a decade of elected legislative record versus a first-time candidate with staff-level and private-sector experience versus a candidate with no governmental experience [S1][S2]. This is the axis the candidates themselves emphasize most.
2. **Homelessness and public safety approach:** Services-first (behavioral health investment, gun-violence prevention, connecting trafficking victims to services) versus accountability-plus-enforcement (performance standards for homelessness spending, "three strikes" mandatory drug treatment, more aggressive enforcement of public drug use) [S1][S2].
3. **Budget and revenue posture:** Expanding investments and progressive revenue (JumpStart-style taxation as a credential) versus oversight, auditing, and skepticism of asking taxpayers for more without measurable results — highly salient given the county's budget shortfall [S1][S2][S3].
4. **Theory of governance:** Conventional representation (both Mosqueda and Duda) versus Jacobson's direct-democracy proceduralism (parallel public forums, interactive pin-drop maps); this axis separates Jacobson from both opponents but does not differentiate the two front-runners [S1].
5. **Coalition and endorsement base:** Institutional Democratic/labor coalition (Mosqueda) versus newspaper-editorial/good-government lane (Duda) versus none (Jacobson) — a proxy for how each would govern and with whom [S1][S2][S3].

Note: partisan labels do not appear on the ballot (county offices are nonpartisan), and no candidate lists a party preference in the pamphlet [S1]; Duda's site quotes the Seattle Times calling him "a common sense Democrat," and Mosqueda's coalition is the regional Democratic establishment, so this race differentiates within a broadly Democratic-leaning field rather than across parties [S2][S3].

## justice-position-no-3-supreme-court  (depth: deep)
- **Judicial philosophy — the clearest axis:** Stevens is a self-described strict constructionist who would read statutes literally and interpret the constitution by text and original meaning; Diaz espouses "American pragmatism," law meeting "the community where they are"; Hawk stakes a middle framing of constitution, statute, and precedent "not personal views," with openness to reform [S2].

- **Appointment vs. election and partisan alignment:** Stevens runs against the gubernatorial-appointment pipeline and holds Republican party recommendations; Diaz and Hawk were both appointed by Democratic governors and split the Democratic-aligned establishment (Diaz: former governors and most justices; Hawk: the sitting governor) [S1][S2][S3].

## justice-position-no-5-supreme-court  (depth: deep)

  Miller: a 40-year Washington appellate practice (135 appeals) with no
  judicial office beyond pro tem service [S4]. Larson: 18 years as an elected
  municipal-court trial judge plus 23 years as a trial lawyer [S4]. Amamilo:
  a sitting superior-court judge handling felony, civil, family, and
  dependency dockets, formerly a public defender [S4]. Voters are effectively
  choosing which kind of experience — appellate craft, trial-bench
  management, or current trial-court judging — belongs on an appellate court.
- **Posture toward the current court.** Larson runs explicitly as a critic of
  recent decisions (e.g., calling the Blake decision ideologically motivated
  and opposing proposed bail rules); Amamilo emphasizes precedent and
  stability; Angelis emphasizes judicial independence and rule of law; Miller
  emphasizes error-correction and getting decisions right [S2][S4].
- **Bar evaluations.** Amamilo reports "Exceptionally Well Qualified" ratings
  from five minority/specialty bar associations and "Well Qualified" from the
  Latina/o Bar Association [S4]. Angelis is rated "Exceptionally Well
  Qualified" by Washington Women Lawyers [S5, see dossier]. Miller and Larson
  cite attorney peer ratings (Martindale-Hubbell AV Preeminent) rather than
  2026 judicial-evaluation ratings [S4].
- **Incumbency by appointment.** Angelis has institutional support (all
  colleagues, three governors) but a bench record of only a few months;
  challengers argue the seat should be filled by election rather than
  ratification of an appointment [S2][S4].

## municipal-court-judge-position-no-5-city-of-seattle  (depth: deep)
- **Prosecution vs. public defense.** The defining difference: Newsom's
  career prosecuting and representing city governments vs. Rothstein's
  career defending indigent clients. In a court whose entire criminal docket
  is misdemeanors, this shapes instincts on charging credibility, release,
  and sentencing [S1][S3].

  Municipal Court itself with decades of courtroom practice behind it;
  Rothstein's pro tem service is newer (2024) but spans six courts and
  includes the jail and impound calendars this court runs [S1][S3].
- **Not differentiating:** both self-report "Exceptionally Well Qualified"
  ratings from minority bar associations (neither set independently
  verified), both emphasize dignity and being heard, and partisan inference
  is inappropriate for this nonpartisan judicial office [S1][S3].

## city-of-black-diamond-proposition-no-1
- Cost vs. service model: the measure turns on whether voters accept a significantly higher, permanent fire tax (levy + non-voted benefit charge) in exchange for membership in a large regional fire authority versus continuing lower-cost contracted service [S2][S3].
- Local control vs. regionalization: annexation trades a city-negotiated contract for a seat on a multi-city regional governing board, with future rates partly set by the authority rather than by city contract negotiation [S1][S2].
- Trust in city fiscal handling: the con case hinges on the claim that the city will retain the tax revenue it currently spends on the fire contract, effectively a double payment; the pro case does not address disposition of existing city revenues [S2].
- Risk tolerance: rejecting annexation leaves service after 2027 dependent on renegotiating with MVFR or PSRFA, an outcome with cost and insurance-rating uncertainty [S1][S3].

## city-of-covington-proposition-no-1
- Tax appetite vs. amenity level: the core trade-off is a new, permanent property tax versus dedicated funding for park maintenance, expansion, and a future aquatic/community center [S1][S2].
- Dedicated funding vs. budget flexibility: supporters value legally ring-fenced park money; opponents see a new district as ratcheting taxation upward with council-controlled rate authority up to double the initial rate without another vote [S1][S2].
- Governance overlap: the "district" is governed by the sitting city council ex officio, so this is less about new governance and more about creating separate taxing capacity for an existing government [S1].
- Timing and affordability: the con case rests largely on cumulative tax burden and economic timing rather than opposition to parks themselves [S2].

## city-of-seattle-proposition-no-1
- Tax appetite vs. service level: the choice is between a ~$0.23/$1,000 property tax (≈$193/year median homeowner) and reduced library funding, since the levy covers roughly a third of the library's budget [S1][S4].
- Renewal-plus-growth: this is not a flat renewal; the council expanded the package about 17% above the mayor's proposal, so voters are weighing an enlarged levy, not just continuation [S6].
- Levy reliance as budgeting practice: the measure continues Seattle's pattern of funding core services through voter-approved lid lifts layered over the 1%-capped base levy [S1].

## king-county-fire-protection-district-no-43-proposition-no-1
- Governance vs. status quo: the operative change is representation (voting

## skykomish-school-district-no-404-proposition-no-1

## snoqualmie-pass-fire-and-rescue-proposition-no-1
- Fee-based vs. tax-based funding: the measure turns on whether cost

- Continuity vs. change: this continues an arrangement voters have approved
  twice before (2014, 2020); rejecting it would force the district to
  restructure its financing, likely toward its property tax authority [S1][S3].
- Accountability mechanics: the charge is reset annually with a public hearing

  [S1].
- One-sided record: neither a pro nor a con committee filed statements, so the
  pamphlet contains only the district's own explanation [S2].
