## What the office does

A U.S. Representative is one of 435 voting members of the U.S. House. Representatives write, sponsor, and vote on federal legislation (taxes, healthcare, immigration, defense, housing programs), control federal spending through the appropriations process, conduct oversight of the executive branch through committees, and vote on impeachment. They also provide constituent services (casework with federal agencies such as the VA, SSA, and USCIS) and steer federal investments to their district. Terms are two years; there is no term limit. Washington's 7th district covers much of North, Central, and West Seattle plus parts of Burien, White Center, Shoreline, and Vashon Island.

## Race dynamics

This is one of the safest Democratic seats in the country. Incumbent Pramila Jayapal (D), first elected in 2016, has won her past four primaries with roughly 80% of the vote and was re-elected with 83.9% in 2024 (per Daily UW column and Ballotpedia). Under Washington's top-two primary, the two highest vote-getters on August 4, 2026 advance to the general regardless of party, so the practical question is which challenger joins Jayapal on the November ballot — historically a Republican drawing about 15%.

The field is highly asymmetric: Jayapal has raised about $2 million this cycle with broad labor and advocacy endorsements (FEC; pamphlet); Nirav Sheth (R) has raised under $7,000 (FEC); Gwen Kirkland (D) has reported no fundraising and her campaign website is unreachable (FEC; site check); David Blomstrom (Fifth Republic) has no FEC filings and is a perennial candidate whose platform is built on explicitly antisemitic conspiracy claims (pamphlet; his website; Daily UW).

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate these candidates

1. **Incumbency and record vs. outsider status.** Jayapal is the only candidate with any legislative record (nine years in the House, committee and Progressive Caucus leadership, 193 sponsored bills). All three challengers have never held elected office.
2. **Direction of challenge: from the left vs. from the right.** Kirkland runs explicitly to Jayapal's left (publicly owned utilities and grocery stores, hostility to the Democratic establishment); Sheth runs from the right (law and order, funding police, fiscal restraint, energy independence). This left-right axis is the clearest substantive spread in the race.
3. **Public safety and policing.** Sheth (ex-Marine, ex-Lakewood police officer) makes restoring "law & order" in Seattle his lead theme; Jayapal's immigration-enforcement bills (e.g., ICE Out of Our Faces Act) point the opposite direction on federal enforcement posture. Kirkland's platform does not address policing.
4. **Healthcare and economic program.** Jayapal and Kirkland both back universal healthcare and free college; they differ on degree (Kirkland adds public ownership of utilities/groceries) and on vehicle (Jayapal has actual bills). Sheth does not campaign on healthcare.
5. **Immigration.** Jayapal (OneAmerica founder, Immigration Subcommittee ranking member) campaigns against ICE/CBP enforcement actions; Sheth, himself an immigrant, promotes legal-pathway immigration and has reportedly posted content opposing birthright citizenship. Notably, three of the four candidates are immigrants or children of immigrants from India.
6. **Israel/Middle East policy.** Jayapal authored an Iran War Powers resolution and is generally pro-Palestinian (though she voted for Iron Dome funding, per Daily UW); Blomstrom's position (severing ties with and derecognizing Israel) is embedded in an antisemitic platform and sits outside normal policy debate. This axis mostly distinguishes Blomstrom's fringe candidacy rather than offering voters a genuine policy choice.

Axes that do NOT differentiate: climate rhetoric (Jayapal, Blomstrom, and to a lesser degree Sheth all invoke environment/energy but only Jayapal has legislation); "anti-establishment/anti-corruption" framing, which every challenger uses in some form.
