## What the office does

The Washington State Supreme Court is the state's court of last resort: nine
justices who decide appeals from the Courts of Appeals, direct-review cases,
questions of state constitutional law, attorney-discipline matters, and rules
governing all state courts [S1][S6]. Justices are elected statewide in
nonpartisan races to six-year terms; a contested primary sends the top two
candidates to the November general election [S2][S6]. Supreme Court decisions
bind every trial and appellate court in Washington, so a single seat affects
statewide law on criminal procedure, taxation, family law, land use, and
individual rights [S2][S3]. Five of the court's nine seats appear on the 2026
ballot, and news coverage frames the cycle around a pending constitutional
challenge to the state's new tax on incomes over $1 million, which the court
is expected to hear after the election [S3][S6].

## Race dynamics

Position 5 is a special election to fill the remainder of retired Justice
Barbara Madsen's term [S2]. Justice Theo Angelis was appointed by Governor
Ferguson in March 2026 and took the bench in April 2026, so he runs as a very
recent appointee without authored opinions yet [S1][S2][S4]. Three challengers
filed, making this the most crowded of the 2026 Supreme Court races: appellate
attorney Greg Miller, retired Federal Way Municipal Court Judge Dave Larson,
and sitting Thurston County Superior Court Judge Sharonda Amamilo [S2][S4].
Only the top two advance from the August 4 primary [S2].

Resources and endorsements are unevenly distributed. Per PDC filings as of
early July, Angelis reported roughly $280,000-$310,000 raised, Larson roughly
$232,000, Amamilo roughly $36,000, and Miller roughly $22,000 [S5]. Angelis is
endorsed by his eight Supreme Court colleagues, Governors Ferguson, Inslee,
and Gregoire, and the Washington State Labor Council [S2][S4]. Amamilo's
endorsements include Justice Whitener (in her prior race context), former
Justice Yu, labor organizations, and Democratic county/LD organizations;
Larson holds the Washington State Republican Party's judicial
"recommendation" and Forward Party support; Miller lists no endorsements
[S2][S4][S5]. Larson is a repeat statewide candidate (2016, 2020, 2024),
losing the 2024 race by under one percentage point, which gives him unusual
name recognition for a challenger [S2].

## What genuinely differentiates the candidates

- **Type of judicial/legal experience.** Angelis: 26 years of large-firm
  appellate and IP litigation plus three months as a sitting justice [S1][S4].
  Miller: a 40-year Washington appellate practice (135 appeals) with no
  judicial office beyond pro tem service [S4]. Larson: 18 years as an elected
  municipal-court trial judge plus 23 years as a trial lawyer [S4]. Amamilo:
  a sitting superior-court judge handling felony, civil, family, and
  dependency dockets, formerly a public defender [S4]. Voters are effectively
  choosing which kind of experience — appellate craft, trial-bench
  management, or current trial-court judging — belongs on an appellate court.
- **Posture toward the current court.** Larson runs explicitly as a critic of
  recent decisions (e.g., calling the Blake decision ideologically motivated
  and opposing proposed bail rules); Amamilo emphasizes precedent and
  stability; Angelis emphasizes judicial independence and rule of law; Miller
  emphasizes error-correction and getting decisions right [S2][S4].
- **Bar evaluations.** Amamilo reports "Exceptionally Well Qualified" ratings
  from five minority/specialty bar associations and "Well Qualified" from the
  Latina/o Bar Association [S4]. Angelis is rated "Exceptionally Well
  Qualified" by Washington Women Lawyers [S5, see dossier]. Miller and Larson
  cite attorney peer ratings (Martindale-Hubbell AV Preeminent) rather than
  2026 judicial-evaluation ratings [S4].
- **Incumbency by appointment.** Angelis has institutional support (all
  colleagues, three governors) but a bench record of only a few months;
  challengers argue the seat should be filled by election rather than
  ratification of an appointment [S2][S4].
