## Background

Greg Miller (Gregory M. Miller, of Seattle) is an appellate attorney and partner at the Seattle firm Carney Badley Spellman, where he co-founded the firm's Appellate Practice Section in 2008 [S1][S2]. He has practiced law in Washington since 1984 and describes 40 years of appellate work: as of June 2026, briefing in over 135 Washington appeals, more than 80 oral arguments, and 25 amicus briefs in the Washington Supreme Court, with additional work in the Ninth Circuit and U.S. Supreme Court [S1][S2][S3][S4].

His earlier career includes clerking for Judge Ken Grosse at the Washington Court of Appeals (working on about 75 cases), serving as staff counsel to the state Senate Health and Long-Term Care Committee in the 1990 session, municipal and school law practice including for tribal-area districts on the Olympic Peninsula, work as a municipal prosecutor, service as a Seattle Municipal Court judge pro tem, and ten years running a solo appellate practice [S1][S2][S3]. Before law school, he spent five years helping build jail health care systems in rural Washington counties; three of those jails were among the first 16 accredited by the AMA in 1977 [S1][S2].

He holds a BA from Harvard University and a JD from Northeastern University, and carries Martindale-Hubbell's AV Preeminent peer-review rating [S1][S2]. He served more than 20 years on the King County Bar Association's Judicial Evaluation Committee, including a term as chair (1999–2000 per his pamphlet statement), and was a founding member of the Washington Appellate Lawyers Association (2001) and co-founder of the KCBA Appellate Practice Section (2005) [S1][S2].

## Positions

Judicial philosophy and approach (as described by the candidate):

- Says he is running "to help [the court] do justice in each case: fairly, impartially, according to law," and argues appeals are usually the only way to correct a court's error, so appellate experience matters on the Supreme Court [S1].
- Describes taking cases individually without predetermined conclusions, examining "how a case came up, what the statutes are, where they came from," and applying the law fairly [S3].
- Presents himself as independent and "not in anyone's pocket," with no political allegiance in his rulings; his campaign notes he has represented clients across the spectrum and says he "doesn't fit a partisan box" [S2][S3].
- Expresses concern that gubernatorial appointment power, if unchecked, can undermine judicial independence, saying merit-based selection processes in governors' offices may have eroded [S3].
- Argues the court is losing nearly 70 years of appellate experience through retirements and that no other candidate in this race has comparable Washington appellate experience [S1][S2][S3].

## Record

Miller has no judicial record beyond pro tem service; the following are appellate-advocacy outcomes described by his campaign:

- Keene v. Edie: the Supreme Court overturned a 107-year-old precedent he challenged, allowing a sex-abuse victim to be compensated while protecting the innocent spouse's separate property ("has done," campaign-described) [S2].
- Anderson v. Grant County (March 2026): the Supreme Court followed his amicus position, filed for seven small counties, on jail accountability when an inmate dies by suicide (campaign-described) [S2].
- Beard v. Everett Clinic (March 2026): the court affirmed 9–0 in a malpractice case in which he filed for medical associations supporting the jury verdict (campaign-described) [S2].
- Won reinstatement and ultimately a jury reversal of L&I's denial of a house cleaner's occupational-disease claim; won back pay for underpaid sheriff's deputies and a tuition refund for a Vietnam veteran; defended domestic-violence and anti-harassment protection orders (campaign-described) [S1][S2].
- Filed 17 health-care-related amicus briefs for organizations including the Washington State Medical Association, Washington State Hospital Association, UW School of Medicine, and the American Medical Association (campaign-described) [S2].

## Scoring notes

- Frames the race around Washington appellate experience: 135 appeals and 80 oral arguments over 40 years, versus an appointed incumbent who joined the bench in April 2026 and two trial-court-focused opponents [S1][S2][S4].
- Has never held elected judicial office; his judging experience is limited to Seattle Municipal Court pro tem service [S1][S2].
- Fundraising is a small fraction of the incumbent's: PDC filings show about $21,900 across 29 contributions (including a $4,800 self-donation per news reporting) as of July 16, 2026, versus about $309,000 for Angelis [S4][S5].
- No endorsements are listed on his campaign website, and news coverage noted the absence; Angelis lists nearly the full sitting court and statewide officials [S2][S4].
- Positions himself as independent of the appointment process, raising concerns about gubernatorial appointment power in a race against a gubernatorial appointee [S3].
