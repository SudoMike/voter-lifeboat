## Background

Nilu Jenks is Political and Partnerships Director at FairVote Washington, where she works on voting reform and coalition building [S1][S3]. She is a first-generation American whose parents immigrated from Iran, and a mother of two who lives in Lake City [S1][S3][S4]. She holds a B.A. in Economics and Spanish from Wellesley College [S1]. Her civic experience includes serving as PTA Advocacy Chair at John Rogers Elementary, work with Roosevelt Alumni for Racial Equity, and work as a learning coach for English language learners [S1][S3][S4]. She ran for this same District 5 seat in 2023, finishing third in the primary behind eventual winner Cathy Moore [S3]. In July 2025 she was one of six finalists considered by the City Council for appointment to the vacant District 5 seat (Debora Juarez was appointed) [S7].

## Positions

### Housing
- Says she will "build more affordable housing"; champions a target of 1,000 new affordable housing units in the district [S1][S3][S4].
- Supports just-cause eviction laws, rent stabilization, and a tenant right to counsel; emphasizes code enforcement to improve living conditions [S4].
- Supports increased urban density [S3].

### Public safety
- Priorities include improving safety on Lake City Way and Aurora and strengthening gun violence prevention [S1].
- Opposes mandatory treatment conditions for shelter; supports a housing-first approach with "robust, accessible, voluntary treatment," mental health services, and community responder programs [S4].
- Opposes surveillance expansion [S3].

### Transportation
- Cites traffic deaths on Lake City Way and Aurora ("Twenty people killed on Lake City and Aurora in 10 years") and supports protected bike lanes, traffic calming, and free transit for students [S4].
- Supports reliable bus connections to new light rail stations [S3].

### Small business and food access
- Calls the closure of the Lake City Fred Meyer a failure of city planning that cut off grocery and pharmacy access for families without cars; proposes zoning adjustments and farmers'-market support to address food access [S1][S3].
- Supports accessible, affordable permitting pathways for food vendors, storefront security grants, and faster police response to commercial crimes [S4].

### Immigration
- Says she will "protect immigrant communities from federal overreach" [S1]; wants city notification systems when ICE detains residents [S3].

### Climate
- Says she will "move us toward meeting our climate goals" [S1]; The Urbanist describes her as a climate advocate and ties her density and transit positions to climate goals [S3].

### Democracy reform
- Says she will "make sure ranked choice voting is successfully implemented in 2027 so voters trust and understand the system" [S1]; her professional work at FairVote Washington centers on voting reform [S1][S3].

## Endorsements

- Her pamphlet statement lists: King County Democrats; the 32nd, 36th, and 46th District Democrats; Washington Conservation Action; Working Families Party; UAW Local 4121; PROTEC17; UFCW 3000; IBEW 46; Seattle City Councilmembers Alexis Mercedes Rinck and Joy Hollingsworth; Senators Javier Valdez and Noel Frame; Representatives Darya Farivar, Julia Reed, and Liz Berry, "and more" [S1].
- Her campaign website additionally lists The Stranger and the Progressive Voters Guide; King County Executive Girmay Zahilay; King County Councilmember Teresa Mosqueda; Port Commissioner Toshiko Grace Hasegawa; school board members; former Seattle councilmembers Nick Licata, Jim Street, and Mike O'Brien; SEIU-affiliated and other unions; Washington Stonewall Democrats; and Alliance for Gun Responsibility, among others [S2]. (The Stranger endorsement is as claimed by the campaign; not independently verified in this research.)

## Campaign finance

- PDC records show about $109,066 in reported contributions for the 2026 election cycle as of 2026-07-16, the most of any candidate in this race [S5].
- Participates in the Democracy Voucher Program and has "Reached the Maximum Voucher Distribution for the Primary" [S6].

## Scoring notes

- The candidate in this race with the broadest institutional-progressive endorsement base (county and district Democratic organizations, multiple unions, Working Families Party) and the largest reported fundraising [S1][S2][S5].
- Distinct emphasis on democracy reform: professional role at FairVote Washington and a stated priority of implementing ranked choice voting in 2027 [S1][S3].
- On public safety, favors voluntary treatment, housing-first, and community responder programs, and opposes surveillance expansion — contrasting with Kang's police-staffing/enforcement emphasis and Georgakopoulos's enforcement-first framing [S3][S4].
- Second run for this seat (2023 primary, third place) and a 2025 finalist for the vacancy appointment, indicating sustained engagement with District 5 issues [S3][S7].
