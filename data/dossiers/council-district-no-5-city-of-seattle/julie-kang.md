## Background

Julie Kang is a 30-year District 5 resident, educator, and immigrant from Korea [S1][S3]. She holds a BA from UCLA, an MA from California State University, and a PhD from the University of Washington, and works as a director and faculty member at Seattle University; she has also taught at UW, training educators for multilingual and diverse student populations [S1][S3]. She co-founded Pacific International Bank, which focused on Korean-American-owned small businesses and grew to $200 million in assets before merging with Bank of Hope [S3]. Her civic roles include the King County Immigrant and Refugee Commission (co-chair per The Urbanist), the Seattle City Club board, and Seattle Rotary [S1][S3]. She has not held elected office [S3]. In July 2025 she was one of six finalists considered by the City Council for appointment to the vacant District 5 seat (Debora Juarez was appointed) [S8]. Northwest Asian Weekly reports she is finishing a clinical mental health program at Seattle University [S4].

## Positions

### Public safety and homelessness
- Her top campaign priority is "Compassionate Solutions to Homelessness and Public Safety"; proposals include "building back Seattle Police Department staffing levels and moving forward with a second police precinct in District 5," "shutting down open air drug markets," and "expanding access to on-demand substance use disorder treatment" for unhoused people [S2].
- Her priorities page lists using enforcement tools including "the Stay Out of Area Prostitution (SOAP) zone along Aurora Ave. N." [S2]. Note: The Urbanist reported in February 2026 that she disagreed with her predecessor Cathy Moore's support of SOAP/SODA zones and backed City Attorney Erika Evans's opposition to them [S3]; the two sources point in different directions on this tool.
- Says she seeks to "balance safety with privacy and civil rights"; questions surveillance effectiveness (The Urbanist reported she opposed surveillance, citing ICE concerns) while supporting improved emergency response times [S3][S4].
- Pamphlet emphasizes "transparent reporting, coordinated crisis response, and measurable improvements across SPD, the CARE Team, behavioral health systems, and community partners," plus gun-violence prevention through behavioral health, youth intervention, safe firearm storage education, and trauma-informed services [S1].

### Housing and affordability
- Says she will "advance middle housing and transit-oriented development to create mixed-income neighborhoods" [S1].
- Proposes shortening the permit review process and shifting from Mandatory Housing Affordability toward Payroll Expense Tax revenue to fund affordable housing; wants housing growth matched with infrastructure such as completing North Seattle's missing sidewalk network [S2].
- Told The Urbanist she would have supported Councilmember Rinck's neighborhood growth centers proposal [S3].
- Emphasizes caring for existing housing stock and protecting renters from displacement [S4].

### Budget and taxes
- Suggests combining budget cuts with selective new taxes [S3].

### Transportation
- Wants east-west connectors, supports the new Pinehurst light rail station and improved bus reliability (specifically Route 77), and prioritizes safer pedestrian crossings chosen using data [S3][S4].

### Small business
- Supports storefront stabilization, small business stabilization funds, technical assistance, permitting pathways for vendors, and public safety coordination for commercial areas [S1][S2][S4].

### Immigration
- Says she will maintain Seattle's sanctuary-city status and protect immigrant workers and businesses from federal immigration enforcement; says she will "stand up to Trump as he threatens our city's values" [S1][S2].

### Approach
- Describes her decision-making as relying on "data, audits, and lived experience" rather than ideology, and says she considers "all of the spectrum of political views and lived experiences" [S2][S3].

## Endorsements

- The Seattle Times editorial board endorsed her ("Residents who want an advocate instead of an ideologue ought to choose Julie Kang") [S5][S2].
- Her pamphlet lists the 32nd LD Democrats; Senator Jesse Salomon; Representatives Lauren Davis, Cindy Ryu, and Sharon Tomiko Santos; Port Commissioner Sam Cho, "and more" [S1].
- Her campaign website additionally lists Seattle Council President Joy Hollingsworth and Councilmembers Robert Kettle and Maritza Rivera; former officials including Sara Nelson, Tanya Woo, Martha Choe, and David Della; LiUNA; the Northwest Women's Political Council; Moms Demand Action; and various suburban electeds [S2].

## Campaign finance

- PDC records show about $107,058 in reported contributions (201 itemized entries) for the 2026 cycle as of 2026-07-16, roughly even with Jenks [S6].
- Participates in the Democracy Voucher Program; has "Completed Qualifying Process" and may collect and redeem vouchers; she was the first candidate in this race to qualify [S7][S2].

## Scoring notes

- The candidate in this race backed by The Seattle Times and by more centrist-aligned current and former officials (Kettle, Rivera, Sara Nelson, Tanya Woo), while also holding endorsements from 32nd LD Democrats and several Democratic legislators — a coalition spanning the center of Seattle politics [S2][S5].
- The clearest supporter in this field of rebuilding SPD staffing and adding a District 5 police precinct; pairs enforcement with treatment expansion [S2].
- Sources conflict on her SOAP/SODA position (February interview vs. her own priorities page), which may reflect a shift over the campaign [S2][S3].
- Brings finance/banking experience (co-founded a community bank) unusual in this field, and frames her approach as data- and audit-driven rather than ideological [S1][S2][S3].
