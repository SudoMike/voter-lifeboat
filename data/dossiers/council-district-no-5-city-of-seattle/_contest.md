## What the office does

The Seattle City Council is the legislative branch of Seattle city government: nine members (seven elected by district, two citywide) who pass ordinances, adopt the city budget, set land-use and zoning policy, levy city taxes, and oversee city departments including SPD, transportation, and housing programs. District 5 covers North Seattle, including Lake City, Aurora, Northgate/Pinehurst, and surrounding neighborhoods — areas the candidates' platforms repeatedly reference (Lake City Way and Aurora safety, the Northgate-area light rail expansion, the closed Lake City Fred Meyer) [C4][C6].

## This race

This is a special election to fill the remainder of the District 5 term. Councilmember Cathy Moore resigned effective July 7, 2025; under the City Charter, the Council appointed a replacement within 20 days (Debora Juarez, the district's former councilmember, holds the seat on an interim basis), with the appointee serving only until the 2026 election results are certified [C1][C2]. Notably, two of the four candidates on the primary ballot — Nilu Jenks and Julie Kang — were among the six finalists the Council considered for the interim appointment [C1]. Jenks also ran for this seat in 2023, finishing third in the primary behind Moore [C3].

The field:

- **Nilu Jenks** — FairVote Washington political director; the institutional-progressive lane: county/LD Democratic organizations, Working Families Party, multiple unions, and Councilmember Rinck; largest fundraising (~$109k) and maxed out primary democracy vouchers [C3][C5][C6].
- **Julie Kang** — Seattle University educator and community-bank co-founder; the center-lane candidate with the Seattle Times endorsement and backing from centrist-aligned councilmembers (Kettle, Rivera) alongside 32nd LD Democrats; fundraising roughly even with Jenks (~$107k) [C2][C5][C6].
- **Silas James** — democratic socialist, disability advocate, former Seattle Disability Commission chair; the left flank of the race (~$45k raised, no organizational endorsements found) [C4][C5][C6].
- **Dimitri Georgakopoulos** — restaurateur-turned-real-estate-broker running an enforcement-and-business-revitalization campaign; minimal fundraising ($2,050) and no press coverage found [C5][C6].

The Urbanist characterized Kang and Jenks as competing "in the progressive lane" of a race that "could determine council control between progressives and centrists" [C2][C3] — though their endorsement coalitions have since diverged, with Jenks consolidating labor/Democratic-organization support and Kang consolidating Seattle Times/centrist-official support. Top-two primary is August 4, 2026.

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate these candidates

1. **Police staffing and enforcement vs. non-police response.** Kang wants to rebuild SPD staffing and add a second D5 precinct, shut down open-air drug markets, and (per her site) use the Aurora SOAP zone; Jenks emphasizes voluntary treatment, housing-first, and community responders and opposes shelter conditioned on treatment; James goes furthest — CARE team with independent dispatch, restorative justice, poverty framed as the root safety issue; Georgakopoulos wants streets kept "free of drug abusers, homeless encampments and violent offenders" [C2][C4].
2. **Surveillance technology.** James opposes ALPRs, CCTV, and Axon contracts outright; Jenks opposes surveillance expansion; Kang questions its effectiveness and stresses privacy/civil-rights balance; Georgakopoulos has no stated position [C2][C3][C4].
3. **Housing strategy.** Jenks: 1,000 affordable units in-district plus rent stabilization, just-cause eviction, right to counsel. James: social housing at 30-40% of stock, vacancy tax, tenant unions, anti-sweep. Kang: permitting reform, middle housing/TOD, shifting affordable-housing funding to payroll-tax revenue, infrastructure concurrency. Georgakopoulos: critiques concentration of low-income housing in D5 without an affirmative build platform [C2][C4][C6].
4. **Taxes and revenue.** James proposes new corporate taxes, a vacancy tax, and guaranteed basic income; Jenks's platform is spending-oriented but not tax-centered; Kang pairs cuts with selective new taxes; Georgakopoulos is dismissive of "tax the rich" framing [C2][C4][C6].
5. **Candidate profile / experience type.** Organizer-coalition builder (Jenks), educator-banker technocrat (Kang), systems-navigator advocate (James), small-business owner (Georgakopoulos) — relevant because none has held elected office, so voters are choosing among professional backgrounds rather than records [C2][C3][C4][C6].

Axes that do NOT differentiate the top three: all three support sanctuary-city/immigrant protections, more affordable housing in some form, small-business support (storefront/vendor programs), and safety improvements on Aurora and Lake City Way — differences are of degree and mechanism, not direction [C4][C6]. Georgakopoulos is the outlier on most of these.
