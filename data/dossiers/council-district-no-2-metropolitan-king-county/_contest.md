## What this office does

The Metropolitan King County Council is the nine-member legislative body
for King County (about 2.3 million residents, 39 cities plus
unincorporated areas). It adopts the county's roughly $20 billion biennial
budget and sets policy for services that include Metro Transit, public
health, the county jail and courts, behavioral health programs (including
the Crisis Care Centers levy programs), regional homelessness response,
elections, and direct local governance of unincorporated communities such
as Skyway and White Center [C5]. District 2 spans much of central and
southeast Seattle (Capitol Hill, Eastlake, the U District, Ravenna,
Laurelhurst, the Central District, Beacon Hill, Rainier Valley) plus
Tukwila and Skyway — one of the county's densest and most
renter-heavy districts [C1][C2].

## Race dynamics

This is an open-seat special/regular election cycle: Girmay Zahilay
vacated the District 2 seat after being elected King County Executive in
November 2025. The council appointed Rhonda Lewis as interim
councilmember; as a condition of the appointment process she pledged not
to run in 2026, guaranteeing an open race [C3].

The contest is effectively a two-frontrunner progressive-lane fight
between two sitting elected officials — State Senator Rebecca Saldaña
(37th LD, in the senate since 2016) and Port of Seattle Commissioner
Toshiko Hasegawa (commission president in 2025) — with first-time
candidate and King County employee Miriam Mboya also on the ballot
[C1][C2][C5]. Fundraising as of mid-July 2026 shows near parity at the
top: Saldaña about $208k, Hasegawa about $202k, Mboya about $44k [C4].
Endorsements split along institutional lines: Saldaña holds the King
County Democrats' sole endorsement, MLK Labor, and the large
service-worker and county-employee unions (SEIU, UFCW, PROTEC17,
Teamsters); Hasegawa has both former governors (Inslee, Locke), County
Executive Zahilay, and maritime/building-trades unions. Several elected
officials endorsed both [C1][C2][C5]. Both frontrunners share broadly
similar progressive goals (affordable housing, transit expansion, taxing
the wealthy, immigrant protection), so the race turns more on experience
type, funding mechanisms, and coalitions than on ideology [C1].

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate these candidates

1. **How to fund social housing:** Hasegawa backs a county property-tax
   levy for social housing now ("act with urgency"); Saldaña is reticent
   about new property taxes, preferring progressive revenue options and
   state-level action. This was the sharpest disagreement at the June 2026
   forum [C1].
2. **Experience type:** legislative record (Saldaña: HEAL Act, Voting
   Rights Act, Domestic Workers Bill of Rights, Labor Committee chair) vs.
   executive/project delivery (Hasegawa: Port terminal expansion, Port
   childcare center, state agency director) vs. internal county
   operations knowledge (Mboya: seven years in county budgeting and
   contracting) [C1][C5].
3. **Transit trade-offs:** Hasegawa explicitly prioritizes preserving bus
   service over fleet electrification if forced to choose and wants to
   accelerate the RapidRide R Line; Saldaña supports a countywide transit
   levy and "free" transit ambitions but was less definitive on the
   service-vs-electrification trade [C1][C2].
4. **Insider vs. first-timer:** Mboya is the only candidate without
   elected experience, running on lived experience (homelessness, foster
   care, immigrant background) and county-staff perspective; she has not
   articulated specific revenue positions, distinguishing her from both
   frontrunners' tax-the-wealthy platforms [C5].
5. **Coalition base:** county Democratic Party + service unions (Saldaña)
   vs. governors/executive + maritime and trades unions (Hasegawa) — a
   proxy for which wing of the region's labor-progressive coalition each
   answers to [C1][C2][C5].
