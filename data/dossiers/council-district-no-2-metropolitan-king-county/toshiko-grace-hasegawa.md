## Background

Toshiko Grace Hasegawa is a Port of Seattle Commissioner, elected to the
commission and serving since 2022, and served as commission president in
2025 [S2][S3]. She is a fourth-generation Beacon Hill resident raising her
children in the home her grandparents bought after being released from a
Japanese American incarceration camp during World War II [S1][S2]. Before
the Port, she led a Washington state agency as a cabinet-level appointee
under Governor Jay Inslee for six years, and earlier worked at King
County's Office of Law Enforcement Oversight on police-accountability
reforms [S1][S2]. She also previously worked as a legislative aide to King
County Councilmember Jeanne Kohl-Welles [S3]. She holds a master's degree
in criminal justice and a bachelor's in criminal justice and Spanish from
Seattle University, and is also a small business owner [S1][S3].

## Positions

- **Housing:** Supports a county levy for social housing ("I believe that
  we need to act with urgency"), building affordable housing, incentivizing
  transit-oriented development, and dedicating county-owned properties near
  transit to affordable housing through community land trusts [S1][S3][S4].
  Has proposed lidding I-5 to create over 1,000 affordable housing units
  plus green space, and supports a public bank to fund affordable housing
  [S3].
- **Transit:** Wants to accelerate the RapidRide R Line project upgrading
  Route 7; at a June 2026 forum said she would prioritize maintaining bus
  service over fleet electrification if forced to choose [S3][S4].
- **Taxes/budget:** Says she will champion "progressive funding options so
  the ultra-wealthy pay their share" [S1].
- **Public safety:** Says she will fund mental health systems and invest in
  community-led gun-violence-prevention strategies; cites her OLEO work on
  police accountability [S1].
- **Childcare/labor:** Points to creating a childcare center and securing
  union jobs at the Port as the model she would extend; says she will
  expand childcare access and protect renters [S1][S2].

## Record

- Port of Seattle Commissioner since 2022; commission president in 2025;
  campaign cites delivering a Port childcare center, union jobs, and
  managing the Sea-Tac Concourse C expansion [S2][S3][S4].
- Led a state agency under Governor Inslee, where her campaign says she
  directed resources to small businesses and communities in healthcare
  crises (campaign characterization) [S1][S2].
- Worked at King County's Office of Law Enforcement Oversight on police
  accountability (campaign characterization; no independent detail found)
  [S1].
- Campaign fundraising: about $201,783 in reported contributions for this
  race as of mid-July 2026, roughly at parity with Saldaña [S5].

## Endorsements

- Elected officials: former Governors Jay Inslee and Gary Locke; King
  County Executive Girmay Zahilay; King County Councilmembers Rod
  Dembowski, Steffanie Fain, and Teresa Mosqueda; Seattle City Attorney
  Erika Evans; Seattle Councilmembers Eddie Lin, Joy Hollingsworth, Dan
  Strauss, and Alexis Mercedes Rinck; State Senator Bob Hasegawa and
  several state representatives (Bergquist, Hackney, Santos, Street,
  Pollet, Farivar) [S2].
- Labor: largely maritime and building-trades unions — IBEW Local 46,
  Firefighters Local 1257, ILWU Local 19, Sailors' Union of the Pacific,
  Boilermakers Local 104, Washington Maritime Trade Council, and others
  [S2].
- Some endorsers back both her and Saldaña [S3].

## Scoring notes

- Executive-branch and port-district experience (agency director, port
  commissioner) rather than legislative experience — the central contrast
  with Saldaña's decade in the state senate [S3][S4].
- The clearest policy split in the race: she backs a county social-housing
  levy now, while Saldaña prefers progressive (non-property-tax) revenue
  and state-level action [S4].
- On transit trade-offs she explicitly prioritizes service hours over fleet
  electrification, a more definitive stance than her main opponent's [S4].
- Endorsement base tilts toward maritime/trades unions, both former
  governors, and the new county executive, versus Saldaña's county
  Democratic Party and service-worker union base [S2][S4].
