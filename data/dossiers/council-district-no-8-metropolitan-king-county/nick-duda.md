## Background

Nick Duda is a first-time candidate challenging incumbent Teresa Mosqueda for King County Council District 8 [S4]. He spent 17 years in the private sector, beginning at large companies including Target and Amazon, followed by nearly eight years helping scale an early-stage data analytics startup [S2]. He then spent about 10 months as a legislative aide to Seattle City Councilmember Maritza Rivera before leaving to run for county council full time [S4][S2]. He holds a BA in Political Science from the University of Notre Dame [S1]. He grew up in Detroit, has lived in Seattle for 13 years (identified as a West Seattle resident), and has two young children [S2][S5]. In January 2024 he was among the 72 qualified applicants for appointment to the vacant citywide Seattle City Council Position 8 seat — the seat Mosqueda vacated when she joined the County Council [S5].

## Positions

- **Accountability and oversight:** Core message is that county government should "return to its core responsibilities: accountability, affordability, public safety, and effective services," with stronger oversight of county departments, programs "measured by outcomes, not political messaging," transparency, and independent auditing [S1]. Told the Seattle Times that expanded social programs funded by increased taxes "should [be held] to the highest standards" with measurable outcomes [S4].
- **Homelessness:** Calls for a "more balanced approach," saying "compassion without accountability is not a strategy"; prioritizes treatment, recovery, safer public spaces, and measurable performance standards for regional homelessness spending [S1]. Supports greater financial oversight of the King County Regional Homelessness Authority and the county's Department of Community and Human Services [S4].
- **Public safety and drugs:** Cites open-air drug use at 12th Ave S and S Jackson St and sex trafficking on Aurora Avenue N as public-policy failures [S4]. Favors more aggressive law enforcement on public drug use, including a "three strikes" approach eventually leading to mandatory drug treatment [S4]. Proposes using available housing space at the Patricia H. Clark Children and Family Justice Center as a safe haven for trafficked young girls [S4].
- **Affordability and taxes:** Says families are squeezed by rising housing costs, taxes, insurance, and everyday expenses, and that government frustrates "willing taxpayers by asking for more and delivering too little" [S1].
- **Fiscal framing:** Says his concern about local fiscal mismanagement is informed by growing up in Detroit and seeing that city's decline; argues the region is "flirting with that tipping point pretty recklessly" [S2].

## Record

- Not a current or former elected official; no legislative voting record exists. As a legislative aide to Councilmember Rivera, he helped shepherd the legislation creating the proposed $480 million Seattle Public Library levy on the August 2026 ballot and conducted internal analyses of the city's public safety and human services initiatives [S4].
- Campaign finance: PDC records as of 2026-07-16 show approximately $13,043 in itemized contributions across 32 records and approximately $3,559 in expenditures for his 2026 campaign [S3].

## Endorsements

- The Seattle Times editorial board endorsed Duda in the 2026 primary, calling him "voters' better choice" for an "innovative and pragmatic approach"; his campaign site quotes the endorsement describing him as "a common sense Democrat" [S4][S2].

## Scoring notes

- The race's principal challenger: a political newcomer running on fiscal oversight, measurable outcomes, and auditing against a long-tenured incumbent [S1][S4].
- Distinctly more enforcement-oriented on public drug use and street disorder than the incumbent (e.g., "three strikes" to mandatory treatment), while still framing treatment and recovery as goals [S4].
- Brings private-sector data/budget-analysis background plus under a year of City Hall staff experience — his depth of governing experience is the main contrast with, and question mark against, the incumbent [S2][S4].
- Holds the race's one major institutional endorsement outside the Democratic/labor network (Seattle Times editorial board); no union, party-organization, or elected-official endorsements were found [S4][S2].
- Fundraising is roughly one-tenth of the incumbent's [S3].
