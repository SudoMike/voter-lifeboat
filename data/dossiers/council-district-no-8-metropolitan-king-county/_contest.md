## What this office does

The Metropolitan King County Council is the legislative branch of King County government: nine members elected by district (nonpartisan on the ballot) who adopt the county's biennial budget, set countywide policy, and oversee county agencies. Its jurisdiction includes public health (Seattle & King County), behavioral health, the Metro transit system, the Sheriff's Office and courts for unincorporated areas, regional homelessness and housing investments, and land use for unincorporated communities. Councilmembers also sit on regional bodies; the District 8 incumbent chairs the county Board of Health and serves on the Sound Transit Board. District 8 covers Burien, White Center, West Seattle, South Park, the Duwamish Valley, Tukwila, and Vashon-Maury Island [S3].

## Race dynamics

This is an incumbent-defense race with an unusual even-year timing: a 2022 charter amendment moved county offices to even-year elections beginning in 2026, so Teresa Mosqueda (first elected 2023) faces voters again after roughly two and a half years [S3]. Three candidates are on the August 4, 2026 top-two primary ballot [S1].

- **Teresa Mosqueda** is a labor-progressive incumbent with seven prior years on the Seattle City Council, near-total consolidation of Democratic, labor, and advocacy endorsements (Gov. Ferguson, AG Brown, Rep. Jayapal, MLK Labor, SEIU, and more), and roughly $125,000 raised [S1][S3][S4].
- **Nick Duda** is a political newcomer from the private sector (Target, Amazon, a data-analytics startup) with 10 months as a legislative aide to Seattle Councilmember Maritza Rivera. He runs on fiscal oversight, outcome measurement, and a firmer line on public drug use and street disorder, and won the race's one counter-establishment institutional endorsement: the Seattle Times editorial board [S1][S2]. He has raised about $13,000 [S4].
- **Mia Jacobson** is a longshoreman and repeat protest-style candidate (single-digit finishes in 2023 and 2025 Seattle primaries) running a process-focused direct-democracy platform with no detected fundraising [S1][S2][S4].

The Seattle Times framed the contest as a fresh-approach challenger versus a veteran politician, crediting Duda with budget-analysis skills the county needs amid a large biennial budget shortfall and oversight problems at the Regional Homelessness Authority and the Department of Community and Human Services, while faulting Mosqueda for a nonresponsive answer on Aurora Avenue sex trafficking [S2]. A side note: Duda applied in January 2024 for appointment to the very Seattle council seat Mosqueda vacated [S5].

## Differentiating issue axes

1. **Experience vs. outsider change:** An incumbent with a decade of elected legislative record versus a first-time candidate with staff-level and private-sector experience versus a candidate with no governmental experience [S1][S2]. This is the axis the candidates themselves emphasize most.
2. **Homelessness and public safety approach:** Services-first (behavioral health investment, gun-violence prevention, connecting trafficking victims to services) versus accountability-plus-enforcement (performance standards for homelessness spending, "three strikes" mandatory drug treatment, more aggressive enforcement of public drug use) [S1][S2].
3. **Budget and revenue posture:** Expanding investments and progressive revenue (JumpStart-style taxation as a credential) versus oversight, auditing, and skepticism of asking taxpayers for more without measurable results — highly salient given the county's budget shortfall [S1][S2][S3].
4. **Theory of governance:** Conventional representation (both Mosqueda and Duda) versus Jacobson's direct-democracy proceduralism (parallel public forums, interactive pin-drop maps); this axis separates Jacobson from both opponents but does not differentiate the two front-runners [S1].
5. **Coalition and endorsement base:** Institutional Democratic/labor coalition (Mosqueda) versus newspaper-editorial/good-government lane (Duda) versus none (Jacobson) — a proxy for how each would govern and with whom [S1][S2][S3].

Note: partisan labels do not appear on the ballot (county offices are nonpartisan), and no candidate lists a party preference in the pamphlet [S1]; Duda's site quotes the Seattle Times calling him "a common sense Democrat," and Mosqueda's coalition is the regional Democratic establishment, so this race differentiates within a broadly Democratic-leaning field rather than across parties [S2][S3].
