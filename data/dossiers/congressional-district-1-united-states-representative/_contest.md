## What the office does

A U.S. Representative serves a two-year term in the U.S. House, one of 435
members. The House writes and votes on federal law, originates revenue
(tax) bills, controls federal spending through appropriations, confirms no
appointments but holds impeachment power, and conducts oversight of the
executive branch. Members serve on committees where most legislative work
happens; a member's committee assignments (e.g., Ways and Means, which
handles taxes, trade, Social Security, and Medicare) largely determine
where they can move policy. Representatives also run constituent-service
operations (help with federal agencies, veterans' benefits, immigration
casework) and steer federal grants and district projects.

Washington's 1st Congressional District spans parts of King and Snohomish
counties, including suburbs and exurbs northeast of Seattle (e.g.,
Redmond-area, Kirkland-area precincts north through Bothell, Monroe, and
Arlington). Only the King County portion appears in this pamphlet.

## Race dynamics

- Seven candidates advance-to-two under Washington's top-two primary
  (August 4, 2026): five prefer the Democratic Party (DelBene, Kincaid,
  Nickel, Gordon, Hildebrand), one the Republican Party (Silva), one the
  Independent Party (Etzkorn).
- Suzan DelBene is a seven-term incumbent (since 2012), sits on Ways and
  Means, chaired the New Democrat Coalition, and chairs the Democratic
  Congressional Campaign Committee — a national party role she holds while
  defending this seat. She has an overwhelming resource advantage
  (~$2.74M raised, ~$1.37M cash on hand through Q1 2026 per FEC) and an
  institutional endorsement list (Sen. Murray, Gov. Ferguson, county
  Democratic parties, major unions).
- No challenger reported more than ~$69K raised (Hunter Gordon, mostly
  candidate loans); several reported under $3K or filed nothing with the
  FEC. None has significant institutional endorsements.
- The practical question for most voters is which challenger joins
  DelBene on the November ballot: the sole Republican (Silva), the sole
  independent with elected experience (Etzkorn), or one of the four
  Democratic challengers running to DelBene's left or right flank.
- The district is safely Democratic in recent cycles, so the primary is
  likely the decisive contest.

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate these candidates

1. **Incumbency and record vs. outsider status.** DelBene runs on a
   13-year legislative record (Child Tax Credit expansion, housing supply
   legislation, trade/tariff bills). Every other candidate runs explicitly
   against career politicians; Etzkorn (school board) is the only
   challenger with any elected experience.
2. **Health care system design.** Medicare for All (Gordon, Nickel,
   Hildebrand, Silva-adjacent no — Silva emphasizes "health freedom" and
   anti-monopoly instead) vs. strengthening the ACA/Medicaid and lowering
   premiums within the current system (DelBene) vs. pilot-tested
   incrementalism before any national single-payer rollout (Kincaid).
3. **Economic left-right span within the Democratic field.** Gordon,
   Nickel, and Hildebrand propose wealth taxes, national rent
   stabilization/control, UBI or jobs guarantees, and (Nickel)
   nationalizing AI/automation; Kincaid pairs a large working-class income
   tax cut with "socialism doesn't work" and social positions to the
   incumbent's right; DelBene favors targeted tax credits and bipartisan
   housing-supply incentives.
4. **Party system itself.** Etzkorn's central pitch is anti-two-party
   structural reform (term limits, dark-money bans, insider-trading bans);
   Hildebrand and Gordon share the anti-corporate-money theme but within
   the Democratic party; DelBene chairs a national party committee.
5. **Tech and AI.** Gordon would halt data-center construction and
   regulate AI; Nickel would nationalize AI; Kincaid treats AI-era energy
   demand as a housing/energy manufacturing opportunity; Silva emphasizes
   cybersecurity investment; DelBene (former Microsoft executive) sponsors
   data-privacy legislation.
6. **Israel/foreign policy.** Gordon would end all U.S. aid to Israel and
   refuses AIPAC money (as does Kincaid refuse corporate PAC money);
   Kincaid supports Ukraine and "zero tolerance for antisemitism";
   DelBene's pamphlet focuses on domestic policy.
