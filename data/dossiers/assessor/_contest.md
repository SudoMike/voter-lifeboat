## What the office does

The King County Assessor values real and personal property in King County —
roughly 750,000 residential and commercial parcels annually, per candidate
statements — and administers property-tax relief programs (senior, disabled,
and disabled-veteran exemptions) and the valuation side of the appeals
process [S1][S4]. The Assessor does **not** set property-tax rates: rates
come from levies approved by the Legislature, voters, and local taxing
districts (schools, fire, cities). The Assessor's numbers determine how the
total levy burden is distributed across property owners, and the office
reviews taxing-district funding requests for accuracy and lawfulness
[S1][S4]. Practical stakes for voters are therefore accuracy and uniformity
of valuations, backlog/processing speed for exemption applications, and the
fairness and accessibility of appeals — not the overall level of taxation,
which the Assessor can only influence by advocating in Olympia.

## Race dynamics

This is an open seat under unusual circumstances. Incumbent John Arthur
Wilson was arrested in June 2025 on investigation of domestic-violence
stalking and violating a protection order; the King County Council
unanimously expressed no confidence and later (April 2026) sent a formal
letter signed by all nine members calling for his immediate resignation;
he was formally charged in March 2026 and is not seeking reelection
[S2][S3]. As a result, every candidate is running on some version of
restoring trust, transparency, and functionality to the office, and several
statements reference the "leadership crisis" directly [S4].

The field splits into two insiders and two outsiders. Al Dams is the
office's own Chief Deputy Assessor (11 years in the #2 job), running on
continuity and technical competence [S4]. Rob Foxcurran is a Certified
General appraiser (Senior Appraiser, City of Seattle; former hearing
examiner on the county Board of Appeals and Equalization) running as a
labor-backed reformer from within the profession [S4]. Christopher Roberts
is a 16-17 year Shoreline councilmember and two-term mayor running on
general-government leadership and endorsements from prominent Democratic
officials [S4]. Dominique Scarimbolo is a small-business owner and Citizens
Alliance for Property Rights chapter president running as an anti-system
critic who argues assessments violate the state constitution's uniformity
requirement [S4]. Fundraising as of mid-July 2026: Foxcurran ~$59k,
Dams ~$46k, Roberts ~$40k, Scarimbolo mini-reporting (under-threshold) [S5].
Endorsements likewise split: Foxcurran holds the King County Democrats,
multiple LD Democratic organizations, PROTEC17, and a Seattle Times
editorial endorsement; Roberts lists Executive Girmay Zahilay, former
Assessor Lloyd Hara, and Rep. Suzan DelBene; Dams lists individual county
and city officials; Scarimbolo has no major institutional endorsements
found [S4][S5].

## Issue axes that genuinely differentiate the candidates

- **Insider continuity vs. outside reform.** Dams argues the office's
  professional staff are sound and experience prevents costly mistakes;
  Roberts argues the failure is at the leadership level and calls for a
  citizen oversight committee; Foxcurran argues the system structurally
  favors large corporate appellants; Scarimbolo argues the valuation system
  itself is non-uniform and unlawful [S4]. Where a candidate locates the
  problem (leadership, structure, or the system itself) is the clearest
  dividing line.
- **Direct valuation expertise.** Foxcurran (certified appraiser) and Dams
  (managing the appraisal operation) have hands-on assessment credentials;
  Roberts offers elected-executive and budget experience plus IAAO
  membership; Scarimbolo offers taxpayer-side appeal experience [S4].
- **Legislative agenda in Olympia.** Foxcurran would push to lift the 1%
  levy-growth cap, add homestead protections, and create a renter's tax
  credit; Roberts and Dams emphasize expanding existing exemption programs;
  Scarimbolo emphasizes audits and uniformity enforcement rather than new
  relief programs [S4]. Note the Assessor can advocate for but not enact
  any of these.
- **Equity framing of the tax burden.** Foxcurran centers a
  corporate-vs-working-people burden-shift argument; Scarimbolo centers a
  taxpayer-rights/due-process argument; Dams centers administrative
  accuracy; Roberts centers customer service, backlog reduction, and tribal
  liaison/oversight structures [S4].
- **Not differentiating:** all four candidates say property-tax pressure on
  homeowners is too high and all promise transparency and better customer
  service; those themes do not distinguish anyone in this field [S4].
