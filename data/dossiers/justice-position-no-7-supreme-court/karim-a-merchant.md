## Background

Karim A. Merchant is a criminal defense attorney with an 18-year legal career, the first 15 years of which he spent as a public defender in King County; since 2023 he has operated his own practice [S1]. He is challenging incumbent Chief Justice Debra Stephens for Position 7 on the Washington Supreme Court; family law attorney David Shelvey and tax attorney Todd Bloom are also in the August primary [S3]. He holds a J.D. from Howard University School of Law (2007), a B.A. from the University of Washington (2003), and an A.A. from Bellevue Community College (2001), and attended Bellevue public schools from 2nd through 12th grade [S1]. He was born in Cleveland, Ohio to working-class immigrant parents and has lived in Washington since 1987 [S1][S2]. He is a member of the Washington Defender Association and the Washington Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers [S1]. His listed community service includes volunteer coaching for youth sports and civics/constitutional-law education [S1]. His campaign website (win4wa.com, which redirects to karim4wa.com) lists a Bellevue campaign address [S2].

## Positions

Framed as judicial philosophy and approach (this is a nonpartisan judicial race):

- States his judicial philosophy as "follow the Constitution," and says courts should interpret law as written rather than inserting subjective intent [S3].
- Says he has represented "people from every walk of life" and has never represented corporations or the government; describes his career as focused on protecting people from unlawful government intrusion, with constitutional protections applying equally regardless of status, wealth, or background [S1].
- Says the judiciary must remain "independent, impartial, and free from politics," and that judges must faithfully interpret and apply the law [S1].
- His campaign platform emphasizes judicial independence, keeping courts within constitutional limits, and public confidence in the courts through fair, consistent administration of justice [S2].
- In a June 30, 2026 Center Square Q&A, he characterized the current court as having an "establishment culture," said in his view it "clearly leans left" and applies inconsistent standards, and criticized justices' participation in Pride flag ceremonies as creating, in his words, "the appearance that issues...have already been prejudged" [S3].
- On specific rulings: he said he disagreed with the court's Quinn decision upholding the capital gains tax, arguing capital gains constitute income under longstanding precedent; he said he supported the Blake decision striking down the state's drug-possession statute as constitutionally sound, placing responsibility for the response on the Legislature [S3].
- His pamphlet statement notes voters "will not find judicial endorsements or a massive war chest" in his campaign [S1].

## Scoring notes

- Career criminal-defense/public-defender background (15 years with King County public defense, solo practice since 2023); no prior judicial service and no judicial rulings to evaluate [S1][S3].
- Challenger to a sitting Chief Justice; frames his candidacy as bringing an outside, anti-"establishment" perspective to the court [S3].
- Publicly stated views on two major recent decisions: disagrees with Quinn (capital gains tax), agrees with Blake (drug possession) — a split that distinguishes him from opponent Todd Bloom, who criticized both [S3].
- Small-scale campaign: 14 reported contributions totaling about $9,800 as of mid-July 2026 per PDC data, and his own statement disclaims endorsements and fundraising [S1][S4].
