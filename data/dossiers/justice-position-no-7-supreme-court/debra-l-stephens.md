## Background

Debra L. Stephens is the current Chief Justice of the Washington State Supreme Court and has served as a justice since January 2008 [S1][S4]. She was appointed to the court in late 2007 by Governor Christine Gregoire and has been elected and re-elected since [S7][S4]. She began a second tenure as Chief Justice in January 2025, having previously served a term as Chief Justice that included leading the court's response to the COVID-19 crisis, for which she received an "Innovating Justice" award [S4]. Before joining the Supreme Court she served in 2007 as a judge on Division Three of the Washington Court of Appeals in Eastern Washington; she is the first judge from that court, and the first woman from Eastern Washington, to serve on the state's highest court [S1][S4].

A Spokane native, Stephens practiced as an appellate attorney and taught as an adjunct professor at Gonzaga University School of Law from 1993 to 2007, appearing as counsel more than 125 times before appellate courts including the U.S. Supreme Court [S1][S4]. She holds a J.D. and B.A. from Gonzaga University [S1]. Her professional leadership roles include co-chair of Washington's Board for Judicial Administration, co-chair of the Judicial Independence Committee of the National Association of Women Judges, service on the Chief Justices Conference Executive Committee, director roles with the National Courts and Sciences Institute, and instruction at the National Judicial College [S1][S4]. Her community service includes past school board chair, the Minority & Justice Commission, YMCA Youth & Government, and other civic boards [S1]. Her honors include a "Judge of the Year" award, the Myra Bradwell Award, and selection as a 2023 Rodel Judicial Fellow [S4].

## Positions

Framed as judicial philosophy and priorities rather than policy positions:

- States her core commitments as "an independent judiciary, courts that are open and accessible to everyone, and decisions grounded in law, not politics" [S2].
- Says that in "an era of growing attempts to politicize the judiciary," she works to maintain the integrity and independence of the courts and to safeguard access to justice for every person [S1].
- Emphasizes that decisions must be based on the law and the facts and that every person must be treated fairly and with dignity in court [S1].
- Cites her background as a former constitutional law professor as grounding her respect for legal traditions and constitutional principles [S1].
- Points to a record of expanding access to justice and improving court efficiency and transparency as priorities she would continue [S1].

## Record

- Authored the majority opinion in McCleary v. State, No. 84362-7 (filed January 5, 2012), the landmark en banc decision holding that article IX, section 1 of the Washington Constitution confers on children a positive constitutional right to an amply funded education and that the State was not meeting its paramount duty to fund K-12 education [S5].
- Has served on the Washington Supreme Court continuously since January 2008, including a prior term as Chief Justice during which she led the judiciary's COVID-19 pandemic response, and a second tenure as Chief Justice beginning January 2025 [S4].
- Co-chairs the state Board for Judicial Administration, the body that oversees administration of Washington's courts [S1][S4].
- News coverage of the 2026 cycle identifies her as one of three sitting justices seeking re-election in a year when five of the court's nine seats are on the ballot [S7][S8].

## Endorsements

- All eight other sitting Washington Supreme Court justices, including Associate Chief Justice Charles W. Johnson, Justice Steven González, and Justice Sheryl Gordon McCloud [S3].
- Five retired Supreme Court justices, including former Chief Justice Gerry Alexander and Justice Mary Yu [S3].
- More than 150 active and retired judges statewide [S3].
- Elected officials including Governor Bob Ferguson, House Speaker Laurie Jinkins, Senate Majority Leader Jamie Pedersen, Congresswoman Marilyn Strickland, Spokane Mayor Lisa Brown, and Pierce County Executive Ryan Mello [S3].
- Organizations including the Washington State Labor Council and The Stranger [S3][S1].

## Scoring notes

- Incumbency and experience: the sitting Chief Justice with 18 years on the Supreme Court plus prior Court of Appeals service, running against a challenger whose pamphlet statement reports no judicial experience [S1][S4].
- Authored a widely cited landmark opinion (McCleary on school funding), giving her a substantial, verifiable body of appellate work [S5].
- Endorsement breadth is unusual: every other sitting justice, retired justices, 150+ judges, the governor, and legislative leaders [S3].
- Financial asymmetry: PDC records show her 2026 campaign with about $82,900 in reported contributions and about $14,500 in expenditures, versus zero reported contributions for her Position 7 opponent on the King County ballot [S6].
- The Position 7 race statewide drew multiple challengers in a cycle where news coverage frames Supreme Court races around partisan attention and a pending challenge to the state's new tax on high incomes; Stephens's stated platform centers on judicial independence rather than case outcomes [S7][S8][S9][S2].
