## Background

Hannah Sabio-Howell is a first-time candidate who until recently served as communications director for Working Washington, a statewide labor advocacy nonprofit that grew out of the Fight for 15 campaign; the Seattle Times notes the organization is primarily funded by SEIU [S1][S4][S7]. She previously worked in the Legislature as a legislative aide to Rep. Larry Springer (D-45) and as a communications specialist for the Senate Democratic Caucus, working for five state senators and the Senate Members of Color Caucus [S1][S4][S5]. She holds a BA in Political Science and Spanish from Whitworth University [S1]. She is a renter, frequent transit rider, and cyclist; she describes herself as a queer person of color from a mixed-status immigrant family (Filipina on her mother's side, eastern Washington roots on her father's) [S4][S5]. She has served on The Urbanist's Elections Committee for four years (two as chair, through 2025) and on the Pro-Choice Washington PAC board [S1][S2][S5]. If elected she would be the youngest member of the state Senate [S5].

## Positions

- **Taxes:** Supports the millionaires income tax but argues it is inadequate (revenue not available until 2029); wants a wealth tax and Rep. Shaun Scott's "Well(th) Washington Fund" payroll expense tax; opposes rolling back the estate tax and luxury vehicle tax; criticizes a $550 million corporate tax break originally embedded in the millionaires tax that Pedersen lead-sponsored (later removed by the House) [S4][S5].
- **Housing:** Wants more aggressive statewide upzoning beyond HB 1110, a statewide social housing program, stronger investment in the Housing Trust Fund, and tighter rent stabilization — arguing the current 10% annual cap is too high; cites a need for roughly 120,000 new Seattle homes over 20 years [S4][S5]. Campaign pledges to "reduce rent and put homeownership within reach by building more housing" [S1].
- **Childcare:** Proposes a stepwise path to universal childcare: expanded paid parental leave, home care support for families with toddlers, then universal pre-K — extending public education "from zero to 12th grade"; cites New Mexico as a model [S1][S4].
- **Labor:** At Working Washington, worked on campaigns for gig-worker pay protections and minimum wage/contract rights for domestic workers and farm workers [S2][S4][S5].
- **Immigration:** Supports the ban on law enforcement covering their faces, banning or limiting ICE operations at hospitals, childcare centers, and courthouses, stripping incentives from companies contracting with ICE, and creating pathways to sue ICE for constitutional violations [S4][S5].
- **Transit:** Argues the state over-relies on highway expansion; wants state resources for more frequent transit service, better operator pay, and possibly lower fares [S4][S5].
- **Healthcare:** Wants the state to backstop federal cuts and take steps toward universal healthcare, building on the Universal Health Care Commission she worked on as Senate staff [S5].
- **Public safety:** Wants to revisit police accountability measures rolled back since 2020 (use-of-force and pursuit standards); criticizes the $100 million police hiring incentive program as ineffective [S5].
- **Democracy/process:** Supports even-year local elections and converting the part-time Legislature to a full-time body [S5].
- **Campaign finance:** Pledges not to take corporate PAC or special-interest money and frames the race as "the only campaign in this race not taking corporate PAC money" [S1][S2].

## Endorsements

- Seattle Mayor Katie Wilson [S2][S6].
- 43rd District Democrats (the district party organization, over the incumbent) and King County Democrats [S2][S6].
- Elected officials and figures: Rep. Shaun Scott (43rd LD), Seattle City Councilmember Alexis Mercedes Rinck, State Sen. Rebecca Saldaña (37th), Port of Seattle Commissioner Toshiko Hasegawa [S1][S2].
- Organizations: Working Families Party, AFT Washington, Transit Riders Union, Tech 4 Housing, College Democrats of Washington and of America, National Women's Political Caucus, Run For Something, Washington Bus, FairVote WA, and others [S1][S2].

## Scoring notes

- Runs to the left of the incumbent Majority Leader on taxes (wealth tax, payroll tax, no estate-tax rollback), housing (social housing, tighter rent caps), and police accountability — the clearest policy axis in the race [S4][S5].
- Contrast in vantage point: a renter, transit-dependent, movement-organizer profile versus a 20-year incumbent corporate general counsel; she frames the choice in generational and anti-corporate terms [S4][S5][S7].
- No prior elected experience; the Seattle Times argues she has not shown how she would be effective in a chamber with moderates and Republicans, and that her affordability answers rely on new taxes [S7].
- Refuses corporate PAC money; raised about $95,000 across 245 reported contributions per PDC data versus the incumbent's roughly $388,000 [S3][S6].
- Won the two most notable insurgent endorsements of the cycle (Seattle's mayor and the 43rd District Democrats), unusual against a sitting Majority Leader [S6].
