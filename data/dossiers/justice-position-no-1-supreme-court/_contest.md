## What this office does

The Washington Supreme Court is the state's court of last resort: nine justices, elected statewide in nonpartisan races to six-year terms, decide appeals that set binding precedent on the state constitution and state law [S3][S4]. Beyond deciding cases, the court oversees the procedures and administration of the state's lower courts [S1]. Because judicial races are nonpartisan, no party preference appears on the ballot, but party organizations and elected officials routinely endorse in these races [S3][S4]. The top two finishers in the August 4 primary advance to the November general election [S4].

## Race dynamics

Position 1 is one of five Supreme Court seats on the 2026 ballot, an unusually large share of the nine-member court [S4]. The seat became open when Justice Mary Yu retired at the end of 2025; Governor Bob Ferguson appointed Colleen Melody, his former Civil Rights Division chief from the Attorney General's Office, and she took office January 1, 2026 [S2][S3]. She now must win election to retain the seat, facing two challengers who both position themselves to her right: Scott Edwards, a private-practice tax and constitutional litigator, and Laura Christensen Colberg, a family law attorney and longtime Snohomish County commissioner pro tem who has the state Republican Party's recommendation [S3]. Both challengers make the appointment itself an issue — Colberg calls it a conflict of interest that feeds a perception of a "stacked" judiciary; Edwards argues recent appointments have produced "one of the most progressive courts in the country" — while Melody defends judicial elections as more accountable than lifetime appointments and notes she has recused where impartiality standards require [S3]. Fundraising is lopsided: roughly $205,000 for Melody vs. $53,000 for Edwards and $27,000 (majority self-funded) for Colberg as of early July [S3].

## What genuinely differentiates these candidates

- **Judicial vs. advocacy vs. quasi-judicial experience:** Melody is the sitting justice but has never been a trial judge; her record is as a career civil rights litigator (AG's office, DOJ) [S2][S3]. Colberg has 18 years of part-time bench service as a commissioner pro tem/arbitrator in family law [S1][S3]. Edwards has no bench service but 30+ years of appellate practice including a dozen-plus cases before this court [S1].
- **Judicial philosophy:** Melody emphasizes state courts as guarantors of rights (workplace, privacy, healthcare, education) amid federal change [S1][S3]. Edwards and Colberg both campaign on applying the constitution and statutes "as written" and depoliticizing the court [S1][S3].
- **Insider/outsider axis:** Melody carries the endorsement of every sitting justice, three governors, the AG, and organized labor; the challengers run explicitly against the gubernatorial-appointment pipeline [S1][S3].
- **Subject-matter lens:** Colberg's campaign is built around family law (including fast-tracking family law appeals); Edwards' around tax and constitutional litigation; Melody's around civil rights enforcement [S1][S3].
