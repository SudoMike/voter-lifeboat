## Background

Vanessa Kritzer is a Redmond City Councilmember (2020–present), serving as Council President 2024–2025 and Vice President 2022–2023; she was previously a Redmond Planning Commissioner and has been a Democratic precinct committee officer since 2017 [S1][S4]. She is Executive Director of the Washington Association of Land Trusts and previously worked at Microsoft, the League of Conservation Voters, and the Latin America Working Group; she began her career organizing on Barack Obama's 2008 campaign [S1][S4]. She holds an MPA and MBA from the University of Washington and a BA from Vassar College [S1]. Board and civic roles include Hopelink, chair of the WRIA 8 Salmon Recovery Council, Eastside Transportation Partnership, OneRedmond, Washington Wildlife and Recreation Coalition, and past service as a University of Washington Regent [S1]. She is a working parent in the district [S1][S2].

## Positions

- **Housing:** Supports a stronger state role setting "the floor" on housing production and affordability with local flexibility on implementation; supports state action on landlord-tenant law and rent stabilization; emphasizes that affordability is a regional/statewide problem [S4]. Campaign highlights delivering affordable housing on the council [S1].
- **Taxes:** Calls Washington's tax code deeply regressive; supports progressive revenue including recent taxes on high earners, and signals openness to broader structural reform such as an income tax; also cites Redmond's business head tax as a model for growth paying for public benefit [S4].
- **Climate/environment:** Priorities include protecting forests and farmland [S1]; record includes Redmond's environmental action plan and climate emergency declaration, electric fire engines, Climate Commitment Act funding, salmon recovery leadership, and land-return work with tribal nations [S4].
- **Transportation:** Focus on "first and last mile" access to the new 2 Line light rail — pedestrian/bike infrastructure, circulator shuttles, transit-oriented development — plus basic infrastructure in rural parts of the district [S1][S4].
- **Childcare/schools:** Pledges to support schools and advance childcare and household affordability [S1].
- **Civil rights:** Pledges to defend civil rights; backed a Redmond resolution affirming constitutional protections and funding for organizations serving immigrants and refugees; wants state anti-discrimination and healthcare-access backstops against federal actions [S1][S4].

## Record

- Six-plus years on the Redmond City Council, including as Council President [S1][S4].
- Strengthened Redmond's inclusionary zoning: raised the required percentage of affordable units and lowered income thresholds served from 80% to 50% of area median income [S4].
- Helped advance a permanent supportive housing project in Redmond that had stalled elsewhere [S4].
- Led creation of a new Redmond municipal code chapter on renter protections, in a city where more than half of residents rent [S4].
- Worked on the "Redmond 2050" comprehensive plan and public-private partnerships with Microsoft (e.g., a pedestrian/bicycle bridge over SR 520) [S4].
- Campaign finance: approximately $109,453 in reported contributions across 336 records for 2026, per PDC data [S3]; raised over $55,000 in the first weeks of her campaign [S4].

## Endorsements

- 45th District Democrats, Congresswoman Suzan DelBene, State Sen. Manka Dhingra, King County Councilmembers Sarah Perry and Claudia Balducci, the mayors of Redmond, Kirkland, Woodinville, Duvall, and Sammamish, and 50+ elected leaders [S1].
- Organizations: Planned Parenthood Alliance, Washington Conservation Action, Washington Education Association, Washington State Labor Council, and more [S1]; her website adds Rep. Larry Springer (who endorsed both Democrats), Congresswoman Kim Schrier, multiple state legislators, Sierra Club, AFT Washington, Moms Demand Action, and other unions and advocacy groups [S2][S5].

## Scoring notes

- The candidate with elected experience in this open-seat race: a six-year municipal record with concrete housing, tenant-protection, and climate actions to point to, versus a first-time-candidate opponent [S1][S4].
- Holds the bulk of institutional Democratic and organizational endorsements (district party, labor council, WEA, conservation groups, most area electeds), while her main rival holds the Seattle Times endorsement and the retiring incumbent endorsed both [S1][S5].
- More explicitly movement-progressive on revenue than her Democratic opponent — open to structural income-tax reform and municipal head-tax-style tools [S4].
- The Seattle Times, while endorsing her opponent, called her "a proven policy wonk," but criticized her for failing to name a disagreement with her party [S5].
- Substantial fundraising lead over her Democratic rival (~$109K vs ~$34K reported) [S3].
