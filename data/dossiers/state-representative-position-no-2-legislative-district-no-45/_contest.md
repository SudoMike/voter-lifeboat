## What the office does

A Washington State Representative serves a two-year term in the 98-member state House, voting on state budgets, taxes, and all state law — school funding, housing and zoning policy, childcare, healthcare, transportation, climate, and civil rights — and shaping policy through committee work. This seat is one of two House positions for the 45th District.

## The district and race dynamics

The 45th Legislative District covers Eastside King County communities including Kirkland, Redmond, Woodinville, Sammamish, and Duvall — a tech-corridor area with rapid growth and some of the highest housing costs in the state, long considered safely Democratic in recent cycles [S3][S4]. This is an open seat: Rep. Larry Springer (D-Kirkland), Deputy Majority Leader and the district's representative since January 2005, is retiring [S2][S4].

Three candidates filed: two Democrats — Redmond City Council President Vanessa Kritzer and technology attorney Chandler Torbett — and Republican John P Gibbons, who submitted no pamphlet statement, lists no website, and shows no PDC fundraising [S1][S5]. Coverage treats the two Democrats as the serious contenders, making the top-two primary effectively the decisive contest between them [S3]. Springer endorsed both Democrats [S4]. Endorsements split along institutional-vs-editorial lines: Kritzer holds the 45th District Democrats, most area electeds, WEA, and the State Labor Council [S1]; Torbett holds the Seattle Times editorial board, Springer's individual quote of support, and WFSE [S4]. Kritzer leads fundraising roughly $109K to $34K per PDC data [S5].

## Differentiating issue axes

- **Governing record vs. fresh execution:** Kritzer offers six-plus years of council record (inclusionary zoning changes, tenant protections, permanent supportive housing, climate plans); Torbett, a first-time candidate, runs on delivery, accountability, and "proving we can use this money wisely" [S3][S4]. This is the race's clearest contrast — both self-identify as progressive Democrats with limited policy daylight [S3][S4].
- **Revenue posture:** both support progressive taxation and the new high-earner income tax, but Kritzer signals openness to broader structural reform (income tax, head-tax-style tools), while Torbett emphasizes pairing new taxes with broad-based relief and accepts market-based solutions where they work better [S3][S4].
- **Issue emphasis:** Kritzer leads with climate/conservation, housing, and childcare from a local-government vantage; Torbett leads with affordability, universal pre-K, and AI/technology consumer protections — he is the only candidate centering AI regulation [S1][S3][S4].
- **Establishment alignment:** Kritzer carries the party/labor/conservation establishment; Torbett carries the pragmatist lane associated with the retiring Springer and the Seattle Times' accountability critique of Olympia [S1][S4].
- **Party contrast:** Gibbons provides a Republican option but no stated platform, so partisan preference is the only signal available for that choice [S1].
