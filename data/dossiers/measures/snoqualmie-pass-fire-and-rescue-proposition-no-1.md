## What it does

- Asks voters to authorize Snoqualmie Pass Fire and Rescue to continue its
  existing benefit charge for six more years, replacing the expiring benefit
  charge voters approved in 2020 [S1].
- The benefit charge may not exceed an amount equal to 60% of the district's
  operating budget in any year, and while it is in effect the district is
  prohibited from imposing an additional property tax under RCW 52.16.160 [S1].
- A benefit charge is a user fee, not a property tax: it allocates the cost of
  district services in reasonable proportion to the measurable benefit a
  property receives [S1]. In prior coverage, the charge was described as
  calculated from fire flows, building square footage, and structure type [S3].
- The pamphlet does not state a dollar cost to a median household [S1].
  Passage requires a simple majority (RCW 52.18.050(3)(a)) [S1].
- This is a continuation of an existing funding mechanism, not a new charge;
  the district says the measure maintains, rather than changes, its current
  financing approach [S1].

## Fiscal mechanics

- Duration: six consecutive years of annual benefit charges [S1].
- Cap: the total benefit charge each year may not exceed 60% of the district's
  operating budget [S1].
- Offset: if the benefit charge is approved, the district's statutory property
  tax authority is reduced by $0.50 per $1,000 of assessed valuation, and the
  district cannot impose the additional levy under RCW 52.16.160 [S1].
- Annual process: the charge is set each year, property owners have a right to
  a hearing, and they may petition for adjustments to their individual benefit
  charge each year [S1]. Prior coverage notes the charge is established by
  November 30 each year for collection the following year, after a public
  hearing [S3].
- The board's stated rationale: a benefit charge combined with a lower
  property tax level is the fairest, most stable, reliable, and cost-effective
  way to finance district operations and maintain satisfactory service [S1].

## Arguments for

- No statement in favor was filed. King County Elections notes that no one in
  the jurisdiction came forward to serve on the pro committee [S2].
- The explanatory statement (the district's official explanation, not an
  advocacy statement) presents the board's view that the benefit charge paired
  with lower property taxes is the fairest and most stable financing method
  for the district [S1].

## Arguments against

- No statement against this measure was filed. King County Elections notes
  that no one in the jurisdiction came forward to serve on the con
  committee [S2].

## Context

- The district serves the Snoqualmie Pass area, which straddles King and
  Kittitas counties; this is a small electorate. In the 2020 renewal, early
  returns counted only 20 of 72 ballots [S3].
- Voters have repeatedly approved this benefit charge: a six-year charge was
  approved in 2014 and renewed in 2020 (passing with 100% support in early
  returns), and the current measure would replace the expiring 2020
  authorization [S1][S3].
- Benefit charges are a common financing tool for Washington fire districts;
  the charge shifts part of the funding burden from assessed-value property
  taxes to a fee scaled to structure size and risk [S1][S3].
- No news coverage specific to the 2026 renewal was found in established
  outlets as of the research date.

## Axis notes

- Fee-based vs. tax-based funding: the measure turns on whether cost
  allocation by measurable benefit (structure size/risk) is preferable to
  funding the same services through higher assessed-value property taxes; a
  yes vote trades $0.50/$1,000 of property tax authority for the charge [S1].
- Continuity vs. change: this continues an arrangement voters have approved
  twice before (2014, 2020); rejecting it would force the district to
  restructure its financing, likely toward its property tax authority [S1][S3].
- Accountability mechanics: the charge is reset annually with a public hearing
  and per-property appeal rights, versus a levy's more automatic mechanics
  [S1].
- One-sided record: neither a pro nor a con committee filed statements, so the
  pamphlet contains only the district's own explanation [S2].
