## What it does

Proposition No. 1 would create the Covington Park and Recreation District (CPRD), a metropolitan park district under chapter 35.61 RCW with boundaries identical to the City of Covington [S1]. The district would be a separate municipal corporation with authority to acquire, construct, maintain, operate, and improve parks, community centers, pools, and other recreation facilities and programs, and to levy a regular property tax of up to $0.75 per $1,000 of assessed value, with an initial rate of $0.37/$1,000 [S1]. The Covington City Council would serve ex officio as the district's board of park commissioners [S1]. District funds must be dedicated to park and recreation purposes and cannot be used for other city projects or operations [S1]. Passage requires a simple majority (RCW 35.61.040) [S1].

Cost to households: the city estimates roughly $277.50/year (about $23/month) on a home assessed at $750,000 at the initial $0.37 rate [S3]. The pro statement estimates about $21/month for owners of median-priced homes [S2]; the con statement calculates $148/year on a $400,000 home and $277 on a $750,000 home [S2].

## Fiscal mechanics

- New taxing district, not a lid lift: the CPRD would be a new junior taxing district with its own regular property tax authority, up to a statutory maximum of $0.75/$1,000 assessed value, starting at $0.37/$1,000 [S1].
- As a regular levy, the initial rate would not require future voter renewal; the district's board (the city council ex officio) sets the levy within the statutory cap [S1].
- Initial spending priorities: improved park maintenance; improvements at Covington Community Park, Founders Park, Jenkins Creek Park, and Jenkins Creek Trail; and planning for a new Whole Health Community Center including a new aquatic center [S1]. The city's project list for the first six years includes Covington Community Park sports field improvements ($4.6M), Jenkins Creek Park ($3.1M), Jenkins Creek Trail ($3.4M), and Founders Park ($3.0M) [S3].
- The district could issue bonds payable from district funds for large projects such as a new community center [S1].
- If the measure fails, the city states there is no identified dedicated, sustainable long-term funding source for park maintenance, improvements, or community-center planning; parks would continue competing with police and other services in the general fund [S1][S3].

## Arguments for

From the statement in favor (submitted per the pamphlet by Casey Hogan, Kolawole Ogundari, and Laura Morrissey) [S2]:

- Covington's parks are "central to our quality of life" but compete for funding with police, roads, and other essential services, and park funding "is falling behind" without a dedicated solution [S2].
- By law, 100% of CPRD revenue would support Covington parks and recreation; nothing can be diverted by the county or state [S2].
- CPRD would improve maintenance, expand parks and trails, and is described as necessary to replace the aging aquatic center with a facility including expanded pool space and indoor fitness, senior, teen, and community areas [S2].
- Median-priced-home owners would pay about $21/month, and district funding would unlock matching grant dollars [S2].
- The rebuttal states Covington has no existing parks tax and characterizes the opposition's utility-tax claim as misrepresenting a one-time 2011 utility-tax-connected policy used to build Covington Community Park [S2].

## Arguments against

From the statement in opposition (submitted per the pamphlet by Gary Cavanaugh) [S2]:

- Covington residents already approved a utility tax years ago that was tied to parks; opponents argue the city is asking residents to pay again ("Whose money is it?") [S2].
- The tax would cost $148/year on a $400,000 home and $277/year on a $750,000 home, on top of high gas and property taxes; "it's just really not a good time to be creating another new tax" [S2].
- Once created, the tax "will only go up. In most cases, taxes never go down," and the district could charge up to $0.75/$1,000 even though it starts at $0.37 [S2].
- The rebuttal urges living within means: "Once a tax is created, there it is... Please vote no" [S2].

## Context

- The city's official CPRD page frames the district as the answer to the absence of dedicated, sustainable park funding, with the general fund increasingly strained by police and public-safety costs [S3].
- The initial $0.37 rate is about half the $0.75 statutory maximum available to metropolitan park districts; the city highlights this on its website, which the con statement cites critically [S3][S2].
- The measure follows Covington's 2022-2042 Parks, Recreation, and Open Space Plan and multi-phase development plans for Covington Community Park and Jenkins Creek Park [S3].

## Axis notes

- Tax appetite vs. amenity level: the core trade-off is a new, permanent property tax versus dedicated funding for park maintenance, expansion, and a future aquatic/community center [S1][S2].
- Dedicated funding vs. budget flexibility: supporters value legally ring-fenced park money; opponents see a new district as ratcheting taxation upward with council-controlled rate authority up to double the initial rate without another vote [S1][S2].
- Governance overlap: the "district" is governed by the sitting city council ex officio, so this is less about new governance and more about creating separate taxing capacity for an existing government [S1].
- Timing and affordability: the con case rests largely on cumulative tax burden and economic timing rather than opposition to parks themselves [S2].
