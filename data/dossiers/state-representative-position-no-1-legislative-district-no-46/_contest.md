## What the office does

A Washington State Representative is one of two House members (Position 1 and Position 2, both elected district-wide) for each of the state's 49 legislative districts, serving two-year terms in the 98-member House. Representatives write and vote on state law and the state's operating, capital, and transportation budgets; state-level authority covers K-12 school funding (the state's "paramount duty"), housing and land-use law, the tax code (sales, B&O, capital gains), healthcare programs, public safety and policing standards, higher education, and environmental policy. Washington's legislature is part-time, meeting in annual sessions (105 days in odd years, 60 in even years).

## Race dynamics

- Three Democrats; no Republican filed. The August 4, 2026 top-two primary will very likely send two Democrats to the general, making the primary the decisive contest [S1][S4].
- Seven-term incumbent Gerry Pollet (seat held since 2011) faces his first serious challenge since 2012, from two well-funded first-time-office-seekers: Ron Davis (urbanist activist, near-miss 2023 Seattle council candidate) and Will Dreher (former federal Jan. 6 prosecutor) [S3][S4].
- Money is unusual for a state House race: Dreher ~$262.7K, Davis ~$154.7K, Pollet ~$129.6K in reported contributions as of mid-July 2026 — both challengers outraising the incumbent [S2][S4]. Post Alley reported roughly $100K of Dreher's total traces to AI-industry donors (including Anthropic and OpenAI employees), which Davis has turned into an attack on Dreher's independence [S3].
- Endorsements split three ways: Democratic establishment and labor (Gov. Ferguson, Rep. Jayapal, SEIU/WFSE, tribes) with Pollet; the new Seattle mayor (Katie Wilson) and urbanist/housing movement with Davis; The Stranger with Dreher [S3][S4].

## Differentiating issue axes

1. **AI/tech regulation:** the race's novel flashpoint. Dreher makes aggressive AI/data-center/social-media regulation his centerpiece; Davis added AI consumer/worker protections after Dreher entered; Pollet emphasizes it least [S1][S3].
2. **Housing and urbanism intensity:** all three back affordable housing, but Davis is the movement urbanist (abundance, social housing, transit); Pollet points to TOD legislation passed in 2025; Dreher frames housing as red-tape and funding [S1][S3].
3. **Incumbency and record vs. fresh start:** Pollet offers 15 years of record (JLARC chair, special-education funding, open government); both challengers argue for generational/functional change [S1][S3][S4].
4. **Money and independence:** who funds each campaign (AI industry vs. labor/tribes vs. urbanist networks) is itself a contested issue [S2][S3].
5. **Professional lens:** legislator/public-health advocate (Pollet) vs. housing-policy entrepreneur (Davis) vs. prosecutor/litigator (Dreher) — little ideological distance on taxes (all favor progressive revenue), so background and emphasis carry the differentiation [S1].
